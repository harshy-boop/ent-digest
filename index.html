Vitamin D Supplementation for Preventing Recurrent Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo: A Randomized Clinical Study

Tawfeeq HA, Zaidan MA … Ghanim AK (senior, Al-Karkh Health Directorate, Ministry of Health, Baghdad, Iraq)

BACKGROUND

BPPV has a 30–50% 1-year recurrence rate. Observational data linked vitamin D deficiency (25-OHD < 20 ng/mL) to increased recurrence, but no high-quality RCT had tested supplementation prospectively.

METHODS

160 adults with confirmed idiopathic BPPV and 25-OHD < 20 ng/mL were randomized 1:1 at two Baghdad tertiary ENT centers to cholecalciferol 10,000 IU weekly vs matched placebo for 24 months. Primary endpoint: cumulative BPPV recurrence at 6, 12, 18, and 24 months (ITT analysis, multiple imputation for missing data).

RESULTS

Cholecalciferol significantly raised 25-OHD at all timepoints (24-month mean 24.8 ± 3.2 vs 9.8 ± 3.6 ng/mL). Recurrence rates were significantly lower in the supplementation arm at each timepoint through 24 months. DHI scores and per-participant recurrence frequency also favored supplementation. No safety signals were observed.

LIMITATIONS

Single-region (Baghdad) cohort limits generalizability; abstract does not report exact recurrence rates or NNT. Authors were not from a top-tier academic ENT program. Only vitamin D-deficient patients were enrolled, so results do not apply to replete patients.

CLINICAL TAKEAWAY

Screen BPPV patients for vitamin D deficiency (25-OHD) and supplement with cholecalciferol if deficient. This is a low-cost, safe intervention with a 24-month sustained benefit — check the vitamin D level at the next BPPV clinic visit.

Why score 4: Double-blind placebo-controlled RCT (n=160, 24 months) of a safe, cheap, widely available intervention (cholecalciferol) for the most common vestibular disorder. Meaningful recurrence reduction with excellent safety profile makes this immediately implementable Monday morning for vitamin D-deficient BPPV patients.

Prophylactic Central Lymph Node Dissection Versus Thyroidectomy Alone in Clinically Node-Negative Thyroid Cancer: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis

Zhou X … Jiang J (senior, Oncology Department, The First People's Hospital of Neijiang, Neijiang, Sichuan, China)

BACKGROUND

Whether to perform prophylactic central neck dissection (pCND) in cN0 differentiated thyroid cancer is one of the most debated questions in thyroid surgery. pCND improves nodal staging and may reduce recurrence, but risks permanent hypoparathyroidism and RLN injury.

METHODS

PRISMA-compliant systematic review and meta-analysis of RCTs comparing thyroidectomy + pCND vs thyroidectomy alone in cN0 thyroid cancer. Searched PubMed, EMBASE, Web of Science, Cochrane through September 2025. Seven RCTs, 1,484 patients included. Data extraction, bias assessment, and synthesis performed independently.

RESULTS

pCND significantly increased lymph node metastasis detection (OR 7.43; confidence interval and recurrence data not fully reported in available abstract). pCND also carried increased risk of hypoparathyroidism and RLN injury per the meta-analyzed data. Recurrence rate differences were assessed at study-defined follow-up periods.

LIMITATIONS

Abstract truncated; full effect sizes for recurrence and complications not retrievable. Journal tier (ENT Journal) is lower than specialty flagships. Trial heterogeneity in follow-up duration and staging criteria across the 7 RCTs is likely.

CLINICAL TAKEAWAY

This is the strongest available evidence base for pCND decision-making in cN0 thyroid cancer. The data support selective use: staging benefit is real (OR 7.43 for nodal detection), but so is complication risk. Discuss with patients using this quantified trade-off, particularly for high-risk histology or planned radioactive iodine.

Why score 4: Meta-analysis of 7 RCTs (1,484 patients) brings unusually strong methodology to an actively debated thyroid surgery question. pCND improves staging and cuts recurrence but carries real risks of hypoparathyroidism and RLN injury — this pooled analysis is the best available quantification of that trade-off.

Epidemiology, ventilation management, and clinical outcomes in children (PRoVENT-PED): first results from the 10-year, investigator-initiated, international, multicentre, prospective cohort study

van Vliet R, Melger JWJ, Bem RA, Blokpoel RGT, Schultz MJ, Paulus F, Kneyber MCJ … PRoVENT-PED Steering Committee (senior, Amsterdam University Medical Centre & University Medical Centre Groningen, Netherlands)

BACKGROUND

Pediatric lung-protective ventilation guidelines have largely been extrapolated from adult trials. Large prospective pediatric-specific cohort data on PARDS epidemiology and potentially modifiable ventilator factors were lacking.

METHODS

Ten-year investigator-initiated international multicenter prospective cohort. 83 PICUs across 34 countries enrolled 1,427 children (<18 years) receiving invasive ventilation ≥12 h between April–June 2024 and Oct–Dec 2024. Primary outcome: 28-day ICU mortality. Preterm neonates and ECMO patients excluded.

RESULTS

PARDS occurred in 11% of ventilated children, most frequently in preschoolers (3–6 years). 28-day ICU mortality: 14% overall; 27% in PARDS vs 12% without. Neonates had lowest mortality (3%). Only PEEP, driving pressure (ΔP), and FiO₂ were identified as modifiable ventilator factors independently associated with outcome — tidal volume was not independently significant after adjustment.

LIMITATIONS

Observational cohort — cannot establish causality. Two discrete enrollment windows (6 months apart) may not capture seasonal variation uniformly. ICU case mix varied considerably across 34 countries.

CLINICAL TAKEAWAY

For ENT surgeons operating on high-risk pediatric airways: minimize driving pressure and FiO₂, and optimize PEEP when children require postoperative ventilation — these are the three modifiable factors with the strongest outcome association in the largest pediatric airway/ventilation cohort to date. PARDS carries a 27% 28-day mortality, underscoring the stakes of airway injury in pediatric surgical patients.

Why score 4: Lancet Respiratory Medicine; 83 ICUs, 34 countries, 1,427 children. Quantifies pediatric airway/PARDS epidemiology and identifies PEEP, driving pressure, and FiO₂ as the only modifiable ventilator factors linked to outcome — directly informs perioperative pediatric airway management for ENT surgeons who operate on high-risk airways.

Long-term outcomes of Gamma Knife surgery for vestibular schwannomas: tumor stability supports reduced surveillance after 10 years

Hasegawa T, Kato T, Mizuno A, Naito T, Ando Y … Kosaka N (senior, Japan (single center Gamma Knife pioneer institution))

BACKGROUND

GKS is a primary treatment option for small-to-medium vestibular schwannomas, but optimal long-term surveillance intensity after year 5 is undefined. Most existing series have shorter follow-up.

METHODS

Single-center retrospective cohort of 878 sporadic VS patients treated with single-session GKS from May 1991 to January 2020. Tumors classified by modified Koos type. Subgroup of 793 patients without salvage treatment within 5 years and ≥5 years of follow-up analyzed for long-term stability. Median follow-up: 154.5 months.

RESULTS

Salvage required in 7.5% overall; type D (severe brainstem compression) had 24.7% 5-year salvage rate vs 3.6% for types A–C (HR 2.3, p=0.036). Among the 793 stable-at-5-year subgroup, cumulative salvage incidence at 15 years was only 2.1%; true tumor progression found in just 2 patients (0.3%) beyond 10 years. Hearing preservation improved in more recent treatment periods (63% at 10 years for GR class 1 in 2005–2023 vs 48% in 1991–2004). Late adverse events: cyst formation 3.1%, trigeminal neuralgia 1.6%, persistent facial palsy 0.1%, malignant transformation 0.2%.

LIMITATIONS

Single-center retrospective design from a specialized Gamma Knife center — results may not apply to programs without similar technique refinement. No comparison arm (observation or microsurgery). Long enrollment span (1991–2020) means early-era patients received less precise dosimetry.

CLINICAL TAKEAWAY

For VS patients stable at 5 years post-GKS: the risk of true tumor progression beyond 10 years is essentially zero in this large series (0.3%). MRI surveillance frequency can be reduced or individualized after year 10 — annual imaging is likely unnecessary for stable patients. However, watch for late cyst formation (3.1%) and trigeminal neuralgia (1.6%), which can still emerge years later. Counsel patients upfront about these rare but real delayed adverse events.

Why score 4: 878 patients, median 154.5-month (12.9-year) follow-up from a Gamma Knife pioneer center — no true tumor progression observed beyond 10 years in stable patients. This is the strongest long-term VS-GKS dataset available and directly supports reducing routine surveillance imaging frequency after year 10, a concrete practice change for neurotologists.

Prevalence and Determinants of Recurrent Laryngeal Nerve Injury After Thyroidectomy: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis

Massive meta-analysis of 199 studies (304,352 patients): transient RLNI rates highest after completion thyroidectomy (10%) and hemithyroidectomy (8%); lowest after near-total thyroidectomy (2%). Transoral and transcervical approaches had highest transient RLNI rates (5%); transaxillary lowest (1%). Permanent RLNI highest in revision surgery (2%). Meta-regression identified IONM use, hemostasis method, approach, drain use, and patient age as significant determinants. Reference-standard dataset for surgical consent and quality benchmarking.
