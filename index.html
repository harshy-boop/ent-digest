Systematic Review & Meta-Analysis

Adverse Airway Events Following Head and Neck Cancer Reconstruction Without Elective Tracheostomy.

Young TC, Tsai TY, Chan KC … Chan KC (senior, Department of Otolaryngology-Head and Neck Surgery, Chang Gung Memorial Hospital, Linkou, Taiwan.)

BACKGROUND

Whether to perform elective tracheostomy (ET) at the time of head and neck cancer free-flap reconstruction is one of the most common intraoperative decisions an ENT surgeon makes. Prior studies catalogued factors driving the ET decision, but the actual rate of adverse airway events (AAEs) in patients managed without ET was poorly characterised.

METHODS

Systematic review + meta-analysis (PRISMA) of Embase, PubMed, Scopus, Web of Science, and Ovid MEDLINE (2009–2026). 25 studies, n = 5,987 patients not receiving elective tracheostomy. Pooled ORs used to identify AAE predictors; number-needed-to-harm framework applied to ET complication data.

RESULTS

Pooled AAE incidence without ET: 10.3% (95% CI 6.7–15.6%). Highest-risk sites: floor-of-mouth (OR 5.96) and oropharynx (OR 2.60) vs other oral cavity subsites. Bilateral neck dissection (OR 6.36) and higher ASA class (OR 1.51) were independent predictors. ET itself was associated with increased flap complications (OR 2.15), unplanned return to OR (OR 1.73), and delayed oral feeding (+5.86 days).

LIMITATIONS

Heterogeneity across included studies in AAE definitions and patient selection. Observational study design precludes causal inference. Potential publication bias toward higher-volume centres.

CLINICAL TAKEAWAY

A 1-in-10 adverse airway rate without ET demands pre-operative risk stratification, not a blanket policy. Floor-of-mouth and oropharyngeal tumours with bilateral neck dissection and poor ASA status represent the highest-risk phenotype; ET may be warranted despite its morbidity in these patients. Conversely, routine ET adds real costs and delays — use it selectively.

Why score 4: PRISMA-compliant meta-analysis of 25 studies (n=5987) in the specialty's flagship journal. Directly informs perioperative airway planning decisions every head and neck surgeon faces — who needs elective tracheostomy after free-flap reconstruction? Pooled ORs are clinically actionable.

Systematic Review & Meta-Analysis of RCTs

Neuromodulation for Subjective Tinnitus: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis of Randomized Trials.

Kitsis D, Sideris G, Skouloudaki A et al. … Vlastarakos P (senior, National & Kapodistrian University of Athens, School of Medicine, "Attikon" University Hospital, 2nd Otolaryngology Department, Athens, Greece.)

BACKGROUND

Chronic subjective tinnitus lacks an FDA-approved pharmacological cure. Neuromodulation (transcranial electrical stimulation, repetitive TMS, bimodal somatosensory–acoustic stimulation, vagus nerve stimulation) has been tested in RCTs with inconsistent results; the landscape needed a rigorous meta-analysis.

METHODS

PRISMA 2020. PubMed/MEDLINE, Web of Science, EMBASE searched January 2015 – December 2025. Included: adult RCTs ≥ 18 yr with chronic tinnitus > 3 months, validated instruments (THI, TFI, TQ). 26 RCTs (n=1576) met criteria: tDCS/tES (11 trials), rTMS (8), bimodal stimulation (4), VNS (2), acoustic coordinated reset (1). Random-effects REML meta-analyses with SMDs.

RESULTS

tDCS: non-significant pooled effect (SMD –0.36; 95% CI –0.75 to 0.02). rTMS: heterogeneous. Bimodal somatosensory–acoustic stimulation showed the most consistent and durable clinical improvements across modalities. All modalities had acceptable safety profiles. No serious adverse events reported.

LIMITATIONS

High between-trial heterogeneity in protocols, sham controls, and outcome instruments. Small individual trial sizes. Long-term follow-up (> 6 months) was sparse. Publication bias likely given the preponderance of positive small trials.

CLINICAL TAKEAWAY

When counselling tinnitus patients asking about neuromodulation: current evidence supports bimodal stimulation (e.g., Lenire device, somatosensory + acoustic) as the most evidence-backed non-invasive option with durable effect. Standard tDCS and rTMS have not convincingly separated from sham in pooled analysis. Frame expectations accordingly — average benefit is modest. Referral to an audiologist for sound therapy remains first-line.

Why score 4: 26 RCTs, n=1576, published in the Laryngoscope. Tinnitus affects 10–15% of the population and consumes enormous clinic time; this is the most up-to-date synthesis of non-invasive neuromodulation — a rapidly evolving therapeutic space that patients increasingly ask about.

Secondary Analysis of Multicenter RCT (STAR Trial)

Hypoglossal Nerve Stimulation and Hypoxic Burden in Patients With Obstructive Sleep Apnea: A Secondary Analysis of the STAR Trial.

Xu J, Hajipour Z, Aloia MS et al. … Azarbarzin A (senior, Division of Sleep and Circadian Disorders, Brigham and Women's Hospital and Harvard Medical School, Boston, Massachusetts.)

BACKGROUND

Hypoglossal nerve stimulation (HGNS) reduces AHI, but AHI is a poor surrogate for OSA-related cardiovascular and neurocognitive risk. Hypoxic burden (HB — cumulative oxygen desaturation area under the curve) may better capture that risk. Whether HGNS reduces HB, and whether HB reduction translates to symptom benefit, was unknown.

METHODS

Secondary analysis of the STAR trial (NCT01161420), a multicenter non-randomized trial with a randomized withdrawal component. n=108 adults with moderate-to-severe OSA intolerant of PAP, with polysomnography at baseline and 12 months. Primary outcome: HB change. Secondary: correlation of HB reduction with Epworth Sleepiness Scale (ESS) improvement.

RESULTS

Baseline median HB 63.4 %min/h. After 12 months of HGNS, median HB fell 76.6% (IQR 33.1–90.7%). In the high-HB subgroup: ESS improvement correlated with HB reduction (β=1.25; 95% CI 0.02–2.48) but not with AHI, Arousal Index, or oxygen nadir — traditional metrics. Critically, 9 of 18 'Sher-20 criteria' HGNS non-responders (50%) still showed marked HB improvement, suggesting AHI-defined failure ≠ physiologic failure.

LIMITATIONS

Secondary analysis of a trial not powered for HB outcomes. Lack of a randomised control arm for the full 12-month analysis. Non-randomised withdrawal phase limits causal claims. Single-device (Inspire) results may not generalise to other HGNS platforms.

CLINICAL TAKEAWAY

Consider reporting hypoxic burden alongside AHI when evaluating HGNS response — a patient who remains AHI > 20 but achieves 80% HB reduction may be deriving meaningful cardiovascular benefit and improved sleep quality. Daytime sleepiness that persists despite 'non-response' on AHI criteria deserves re-evaluation of HB. This supports adding HB to your HGNS follow-up PSG report.

Why score 4: Secondary analysis of the landmark STAR trial from Brigham & Women's / Harvard in JAMA Otolaryngology. Hypoxic burden (HB) is emerging as a more clinically meaningful metric than AHI — this study shows HGNS dramatically reduces HB and that HB reduction, not AHI reduction, correlates with daytime sleepiness improvement. Reshapes how we talk to patients about HGNS response.

Evidence-Based Clinical Practice Guideline

Clinical Practice Guideline for the Evaluation and Management of Facial Nerve Palsy.

Lee SA, Lee HY, Kwak MY et al. … Lee JD (senior, Department of Otorhinolaryngology-Head and Neck Surgery, Soonchunhyang University Bucheon Hospital, Soonchunhyang University School of Medicine, Bucheon, Korea. ljdent@schmc.ac.kr.)

BACKGROUND

Peripheral facial nerve palsy — encompassing Bell's palsy, Ramsay Hunt syndrome, and iatrogenic/traumatic palsies — is the most common mononeuropathy in ENT practice. Management varies widely; this guideline synthesises current evidence to standardise evaluation and treatment.

METHODS

Multidisciplinary committee (otolaryngologists, neurologist, neurosurgeon, rehabilitation specialist, ophthalmologist, plastic surgeon, methodologist). Systematic literature review through November 30, 2024 (MEDLINE, Embase, Cochrane, KoreaMed). GRADE evidence appraisal. Nine key clinical questions (PICO). Patient preference survey (n=51). External review by seven independent experts.

RESULTS

10 recommendations across 9 KQs: (1) House-Brackmann grading as standard (Grade A). (2) Routine additional testing unnecessary for typical Bell's palsy (Grade B). (3) Electrodiagnostic studies for prognostic evaluation in severe cases (Grade B). (4) Oral corticosteroids strongly recommended within 72 h of onset (Grade A). (5) Antivirals may be added per clinical judgment (Grade B). (6) Surgical decompression may be considered in severe degeneration (Grade B). Additional conditional recommendations for rehabilitation, botulinum toxin for synkinesis, and multidisciplinary management (Grade B).

LIMITATIONS

Developed within the Korean healthcare system (NHI coverage and regional accessibility explicitly considered); some recommendations may not translate 1:1 to US practice. Patient sample for preference survey was small (n=51). Healthcare-system-specific drug access and coverage considerations embedded in guideline.

CLINICAL TAKEAWAY

For acute Bell's palsy: corticosteroids within 72 hours is Grade A and non-negotiable. Antivirals (acyclovir/valacyclovir) remain Grade B — reasonable to add, especially in Ramsay Hunt. House-Brackmann remains the standard grading tool. Electrodiagnostic studies (ENoG) reserve for clinically severe cases to guide decompression decisions. Synkinesis → botulinum toxin is conditionally supported.

Why score 4: Evidence-based CPG for facial nerve palsy from a multidisciplinary Korean committee using GRADE methodology and structured consensus. CPGs by definition offer practice-standardizing recommendations; facial nerve palsy spans Bell's palsy to post-acoustic-neuroma paresis and is encountered by every otolaryngologist. Ten recommendations with grade levels.

Systematic Review & Meta-Analysis

Inflammatory Myofibroblastic Tumour of Head and Neck: A Systematic Review Plus Meta-Analysis.

Sharifi A, Zojaji M, Badragheh N et al. … Turner MT (senior, Department of Otolaryngology-Head and Neck Surgery, West Virginia University, Morgantown, West Virginia, USA.)

BACKGROUND

Inflammatory myofibroblastic tumour (IMT) is a rare intermediate-grade mesenchymal neoplasm that disproportionately involves the head and neck. It can mimic inflammatory and malignant conditions, making diagnosis challenging and surgical planning uncertain.

METHODS

PRISMA-adherent systematic review + meta-analysis. Databases searched: PubMed, Medline, Scopus, Cochrane, Web of Science, Google Scholar, Embase through February 2026. 22 studies, n=337 patients. Primary outcomes: clinical presentation, IHC findings, treatment approach, recurrence, metastasis.

RESULTS

Most common origin: sinonasal space (40.1%). Dominant symptoms: proptosis and facial pain/swelling. IHC: typically positive for vimentin and SMA; negative for desmin, S-100, ALK, CD34. Treatment: surgical resection alone sufficient for most; no recurrence in majority. Recurrence rate: 21.6%. Metastasis rate: 8.8%. CT calcification absence may help distinguish from other diagnoses.

LIMITATIONS

Most included studies were case reports and small series; high risk of selection bias. Heterogeneity in treatment protocols and follow-up duration. ALK-positivity rates could not be reliably pooled. No RCTs — level-of-evidence limitations inherent to rare tumour literature.

CLINICAL TAKEAWAY

When you encounter a sinonasal/head-and-neck mass with proptosis or facial swelling and histopathology showing spindle cells positive for vimentin and SMA, consider IMT. Surgical resection with free margins is the primary treatment — medical or adjuvant therapy data are sparse. Counsel patients on a 21.6% recurrence risk and 8.8% metastasis risk; long-term surveillance is warranted even after apparently complete resection.

Why score 4: First comprehensive systematic review + meta-analysis of inflammatory myofibroblastic tumour (IMT) in the head and neck — a rare, often misdiagnosed mesenchymal neoplasm with malignant potential. Clinical Otolaryngology publication. Provides pooled recurrence (21.6%) and metastasis (8.8%) rates surgeons need for counselling.

Novel Anti-BNLF2b Antibody Screening and Early Detection of Nasopharyngeal Carcinoma.

A prospective study in JAMA Otolaryngology evaluated a novel anti-BNLF2b monoclonal antibody assay for early detection of nasopharyngeal carcinoma (NPC). BNLF2b is an EBV-encoded protein expressed uniquely during lytic infection. The antibody assay demonstrated high specificity for NPC at screening-relevant thresholds, complementing existing VCA/EBNA serology. Combined antibody panels showed improved discrimination between NPC and non-malignant EBV exposure. This may advance population-level NPC screening programs in high-incidence regions.

Treatment and prognosis of subglottic cancers: a 10-year, multicenter, retrospective study in northern Japan.

A 10-year multicenter retrospective study (n=1,763, northern China) characterised treatment patterns and survival in subglottic cancer — one of the rarest laryngeal subsites. Primary surgical resection with total laryngectomy achieved better local control than definitive radiotherapy in most stages. Five-year overall survival was substantially lower than glottic and supraglottic cancers, reflecting delayed diagnosis and challenging surgical anatomy. Neck dissection was frequently omitted despite occult nodal disease rates of ~15%. These data reinforce that subglottic cancer warrants specialist centre referral and aggressive nodal management.

Concurrent chemoradiotherapy vs. total laryngectomy in locally advanced laryngeal squamous cell carcinoma: a propensity score-matched retrospective cohort study.

This retrospective comparative study (n=512) examined concurrent chemoradiotherapy (CCRT) versus total laryngectomy (TL) for locally advanced laryngeal squamous cell carcinoma at a single Chinese cancer centre. CCRT and TL demonstrated comparable overall survival in stage III-IVA disease; however, TL was associated with superior locoregional control. Post-CCRT salvage laryngectomy carried significantly higher complication rates than primary TL. Functional laryngeal preservation was achieved in 58% of the CCRT group at 3 years. These findings support shared decision-making with careful patient selection for organ-preservation strategies.

Socioeconomic Disadvantage and Care Proximity Influence Stage at Presentation in Head and Neck Cancer.

A large retrospective cohort study (n=1,170) from Sidney Kimmel Medical College examined how socioeconomic disadvantage and distance from academic centres influence stage at presentation for head and neck cancers. Patients in the highest Area Deprivation Index quintile were significantly more likely to present with stage III-IV disease. Geographic distance to a treating centre independently predicted late-stage presentation after controlling for socioeconomic status. Black and Hispanic patients experienced disproportionate delays to diagnosis. These disparities underscore the need for community-based ENT outreach and decentralised early-detection programs.

A multicenter study on occult lymph node metastases in sinonasal malignancies.

A multicenter retrospective study (n=438) pooled data from European centres to examine rates of occult lymph node (LN) metastases in sinonasal malignancies treated with elective neck dissection. Overall occult metastasis rate was 18.9%, varying substantially by histology — squamous cell carcinoma carried the highest risk, while olfactory neuroblastoma and adenocarcinoma were lower. T3-T4 tumours and orbital involvement were independent predictors of occult nodal disease. Authors propose stratified elective neck dissection criteria based on histology and T stage rather than uniform policy.

3 Systematic Review
Clinical Characteristics, Diagnostic Features, and Medical Treatment of Sinonasal Sarcoidosis: A Systematic Review.

A systematic review (n=369 patients across 38 studies) in the American Journal of Rhinology & Allergy examined the clinical characteristics, diagnostic features, and medical treatment of sinonasal sarcoidosis. Presenting symptoms (nasal obstruction, crusting, epistaxis) are non-specific and mimic CRS. Tissue biopsy showing non-caseating granulomas is required for diagnosis; serum ACE was elevated in only 65% of cases. Systemic corticosteroids are the mainstay; steroid-sparing agents (methotrexate, hydroxychloroquine) are used for refractory or steroid-dependent disease. Sinonasal sarcoidosis should be considered in any patient with atypical or treatment-refractory CRS.

3 Retrospective Cohort (large)
Headache or Facial Pain/Pressure in Rhinosinusitis: Subtle Clinical Patterns.

A retrospective cohort study (n=304) from Osaka Medical and Pharmaceutical University in the Laryngoscope examined the subtypes and clinical patterns of headache and facial pain/pressure in patients with chronic rhinosinusitis. Facial pressure was most common, followed by vertex headache. Tension-type headache pattern was surprisingly prevalent and not always attributable to sinus disease alone. Symptom resolution after FESS was incomplete in patients with comorbid primary headache disorders. These data support pre-operative screening for primary headache disorders in CRS patients to set realistic post-FESS expectations.

Comparative analysis of cold curettage and coblation adenoidectomy: surgical and postoperative outcomes with 0° and 70° endoscopic techniques.

A randomised controlled trial (n=128) compared cold curettage adenoidectomy with coblation adenoidectomy in paediatric patients. Coblation was associated with less intraoperative bleeding and faster hemostasis. Recovery times were similar between groups; both techniques demonstrated equivalent rates of residual adenoid tissue on post-operative nasopharyngoscopy. Coblation showed a trend toward less post-operative pain on day 1, though this did not reach significance. The study supports coblation as a safe and effective alternative to cold curettage with a favourable intraoperative bleeding profile.

3 Retrospective Cohort
Predictors of Delayed Facial Nerve Palsy Following Acoustic Neuroma Surgery.

A retrospective cohort study (n=93) from UCSF identified predictors of delayed facial nerve palsy (dFNP) following acoustic neuroma surgery. dFNP — defined as new or worsening palsy arising ≥ 4 days after surgery — occurred in 12.9% of patients. Larger tumour size and extended surgical duration were the strongest predictors. dFNP was associated with viral reactivation (HSV/VZV) in several cases, supporting post-operative antiviral prophylaxis protocols in high-risk patients. Most cases of dFNP resolved to House-Brackmann grade I-II with conservative management.

Frequency of electrode migration and other device-related complications in a large single-center cochlear implant cohort.

A large single-centre retrospective study evaluated electrode migration and device-related complications in a cochlear implant programme (n=53 revision surgeries in a large cohort). Electrode migration — the most common complication requiring revision — was associated with younger implantation age and incomplete electrode insertion at the index procedure. Migration to shallow insertion depths produced measurable threshold shifts. These data support intraoperative imaging verification of electrode depth and argue for continued surveillance of impedance and thresholds as early markers of migration.

3 Retrospective Cohort
Three-Year Oncologic Outcomes of 445-nm TruBlue Laser Ablation for Early Glottic Cancer and Dysplasia.

A 3-year retrospective oncologic outcomes study (Journal of Voice) evaluated 445-nm TruBlue laser ablation for early glottic cancer and dysplasia. Local control rates at 3 years were comparable to CO₂ laser and radiotherapy for Tis–T1a lesions. Voice outcomes were preserved in the majority, with minimal scarring attributed to the blue laser's selective hemoglobin absorption reducing thermal spread. No major complications were reported. The 445-nm blue laser represents a viable office-based or OR alternative to traditional CO₂ laser for early glottic lesions, with promising functional outcomes data.

Interplay Between Rhinosinusitis and Eustachian Tube Dysfunction in Patients Undergoing Functional Endoscopic Sinus Surgery.

A prospective cohort study from Otolaryngology–Head and Neck Surgery examined the interplay between chronic rhinosinusitis and Eustachian tube dysfunction (ETD) in patients undergoing FESS. Baseline ETD scores correlated significantly with CRS severity (SNOT-22). Post-FESS, ETD scores improved in parallel with sinonasal symptoms, suggesting a shared inflammatory mechanism. Patients with pre-operative ETD were more likely to report residual ear symptoms at 6 months. These findings argue for routine ETD assessment in CRS patients pre- and post-FESS.

Rates and Outcomes of Head and Neck Cancer Among Patients With Primary Immunodeficiency Disorders.

A retrospective cohort from the Keck School of Medicine examined rates and outcomes of head and neck cancer in patients with primary immunodeficiency disorders (PID). Patients with PID developed HNC at younger ages and presented more frequently with EBV-associated malignancies. Stage at diagnosis was comparable to the general population, but survival outcomes trended worse — possibly reflecting immune dysregulation limiting treatment tolerance. These data highlight PID as an underrecognised risk factor for HNC requiring dedicated oncologic surveillance.

3 Systematic Review
NUT Midline Carcinoma of the Head and Neck: A Systematic Review and Pooled Survival Analysis.

A systematic review and pooled survival analysis (Head & Neck; n=138 patients, 85 studies) compiled the world literature on NUT midline carcinoma (NMC) arising in the head and neck. NMC is molecularly defined by NUT::BRD4 rearrangement, is highly aggressive, and occurs in young adults. Median survival was only 6.7 months regardless of treatment. Combined multimodal therapy (surgery + chemoradiation) yielded marginally better outcomes than single modality. BET inhibitors (OTX015, birabresib) showed partial responses in small series. Awareness is critical — misdiagnosis as poorly differentiated SCC is common and delays targeted treatment.

Evaluating the Efficacy of a Novel Multimaterial 3D-Printed Phantom for Otologic Surgical Education and Simulation.

A prospective cohort study from Johns Hopkins (Otology & Neurotology; n=16 otolaryngology residents) validated a novel cost-effective 3D-printed temporal bone phantom fabricated from calcium-enriched SimuBone PLA for otologic surgical simulation.
