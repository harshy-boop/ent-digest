De-Escalated Adjuvant Radiation Therapy in Patients With HPV-Positive Oropharyngeal Cancer

Hidalgo CM et al. (Mayo Clinic)

BACKGROUND

HPV-associated oropharyngeal squamous cell carcinoma (HPV-OPSCC) is rising in incidence. Standard adjuvant therapy is 60 Gy ± cisplatin (30 fractions over 6 weeks), carrying significant mucosal and systemic toxicity. Prior Mayo Clinic trials MC1273 and MC1675 demonstrated that de-escalated adjuvant RT (DART: 30–36 Gy BID with docetaxel 15 mg/m² days 1 and 8) reduced toxicity and improved patient-reported outcomes vs. standard therapy, but real-world adoption outcomes were unknown.

METHODS

Retrospective cohort study at a multisite tertiary center of 282 patients with HPV-OPSCC who underwent margin-negative resection (August 2013–March 2023) and received DART, categorized as on-study (MC1273/MC1675, n=176) or off-study/clinical practice (n=106). Kaplan-Meier progression-free survival (PFS) was the primary endpoint. Patients with pT0/pT4 disease, prior H&N cancer, unknown HPV status, or metastatic disease were excluded.

RESULTS

Two-year PFS was 93% in both on-study (88–97%) and off-study (88–99%) groups. Off-study patients had fewer pN2 cases (3.8% vs. 11.9%, P=0.02), reflecting interim MC1675 guidance. For pN1 without extranodal extension: 2- and 5-year PFS of 97% and 96%. For pN1 with ENE: 93% and 87%. Median follow-up: 5.2 years on-study vs. 2.2 years off-study.

LIMITATIONS

Retrospective design with shorter follow-up in off-study group (2.2 vs. 5.2 years); single health system with high-volume DART expertise; off-study patients were pre-selected by excluding pN2 disease based on trial data, creating a less-sick off-study cohort; generalizability to centers without DART experience is uncertain.

CLINICAL TAKEAWAY

Real-world DART outcomes mirror clinical trial results in well-selected HPV-OPSCC. Patients with pN1/no ENE are ideal candidates (5-year PFS 96%). This validates routine DART adoption at institutions experienced with the regimen and reinforces that de-escalation does not sacrifice tumor control in the right patient. A follow-up biomarker-stratified trial for pN1+ENE patients is underway.

Why score 5: Phase 3 RCT-validated regimen tested in real-world clinical practice at a high-volume center; equivalent oncologic outcomes with substantially reduced toxicity make this immediately practice-relevant for H&N surgeons and radiation oncologists.

Vitamin D Supplementation for Preventing Recurrent Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo: A Randomized Clinical Study

Tawfeeq HA, Zaidan MA, Ghanim AK (Baghdad, Iraq)

BACKGROUND

BPPV is the most common cause of vertigo, affecting approximately 2.4% of adults annually with recurrence rates of 15–56% at 1 year. Vitamin D deficiency is prevalent globally and has been associated with increased BPPV recurrence in prior observational studies, as calcium metabolism disruption may promote otoconia dissolution and reformation. No adequately powered double-blind RCT had previously confirmed this relationship.

METHODS

Prospective, double-blind, placebo-controlled RCT at two tertiary ENT centers in Baghdad. 160 adults with confirmed idiopathic BPPV and serum 25-hydroxyvitamin D < 20 ng/mL were randomized 1:1 to cholecalciferol 10,000 IU weekly or matched placebo for 24 months. Primary outcome: cumulative BPPV recurrence at 6, 12, 18, and 24 months. Secondary outcomes included serum 25-OHD concentrations, DHI scores, and per-participant recurrence frequency. ITT analysis with multiple imputation for missing data.

RESULTS

Cholecalciferol significantly elevated serum 25-OHD at all timepoints (24 months: 24.8 ± 3.2 vs. 9.8 ± 3.6 ng/mL, p<0.001). Supplementation provided sustained, statistically significant reduction in BPPV recurrence at all four measurement timepoints, with the abstract describing a 'sustained, clinically meaningful reduction.' DHI scores improved significantly in the treatment group. No safety concerns were reported.

LIMITATIONS

Single geographic/ethnic population in Baghdad; may not generalize to populations with different dietary patterns or baseline vitamin D status. The abstract does not report specific recurrence incidence figures, limiting NNT calculation. Twenty-four months may be insufficient to assess very long-term recurrence. Compliance with weekly dosing was not explicitly characterized.

CLINICAL TAKEAWAY

Cholecalciferol 10,000 IU weekly for 24 months significantly reduces BPPV recurrence in vitamin D-deficient patients. This represents level I evidence supporting routine serum 25-OHD screening in BPPV patients and targeted supplementation when levels are below 20 ng/mL. This is a low-cost, low-risk intervention with a strong safety profile that can be implemented Monday morning for all BPPV patients.

Why score 5: Double-blind placebo-controlled RCT demonstrating significant, sustained reduction in BPPV recurrence with a cheap, safe, widely available intervention — directly actionable in every ENT clinic where BPPV is the most common vestibular diagnosis.

Risk Reduction of Recurrent Cerebrospinal Fluid Leaks With Postoperative Intracranial Pressure Management: A Systematic Review

Wilson P et al. (Mayo Clinic Florida)

BACKGROUND

Spontaneous lateral skull base CSF leaks (tegmen, posterior meatal wall) occur predominantly in patients with elevated intracranial pressure, often related to idiopathic intracranial hypertension. Recurrence after surgical repair is common and postoperative ICP-lowering therapy — typically acetazolamide or lumbar drainage — is widely employed to reduce recurrence risk, but the evidence supporting this practice had not been systematically evaluated.

METHODS

Systematic review following PRISMA guidelines. Five databases searched from inception through April 7, 2025. Inclusion: adult patients with lateral skull base spontaneous CSF leaks undergoing primary surgical repair with specified postoperative ICP management. Pooled recurrence rates calculated by intervention type (acetazolamide, lumbar drainage, or no therapy) and compared between cohorts.

RESULTS

36 studies including 687 patients (737 leak cases) met inclusion criteria. Six comparative cohorts (34 treated vs. 155 untreated) showed no significant difference in recurrence rate (risk ratio 1.16, 95% CI 0.14–9.76). Limited observational data suggested acetazolamide may be associated with lower recurrence in some subgroups. Lumbar drainage showed no demonstrated sustained benefit. High heterogeneity across studies was noted.

LIMITATIONS

Predominantly observational, non-randomized studies with high heterogeneity; comparative cohorts were small (34 treated vs. 155 untreated); non-standardized intervention protocols (dose, duration, indication for ICP therapy); selection bias in who received treatment; publication bias likely; no prospective RCT data available.

CLINICAL TAKEAWAY

Universal postoperative ICP-lowering therapy is not supported by current evidence after lateral skull base spontaneous CSF leak repair. Surgeons should individualize the decision — acetazolamide may have a role in high-risk patients (elevated opening pressure on LP, obesity, papilledema), but routine use in all patients is not evidence-based. Lumbar drainage should not be used as a default postoperative maneuver. Prospective risk-stratified trials are urgently needed.

Why score 5: Challenges the common practice of universal postoperative ICP-lowering therapy after lateral skull base CSF leak repair with the largest systematic review to date (737 cases); findings support individualizing — rather than routinely administering — acetazolamide or lumbar drainage.

Systematic Review & Meta-Analysisn=66448

Impact of Molecular Testing on Surgical Decision-Making in Indeterminate Thyroid Nodules: A Global Meta-Analysis Across Test Generations

Nguyen TP et al. (King George's Medical University)

BACKGROUND

Approximately 45.6% of all thyroid FNA biopsies yield indeterminate cytology (Bethesda III/IV), creating a clinical dilemma: malignancy risk ranges from 5–75%, yet diagnostic thyroidectomy carries procedural morbidity. Molecular testing (first-gen: Afirma GEC, ThyroSeq v1/v2; second-gen: Afirma GSC, ThyroSeq v3, ThyraMIR) was developed to risk-stratify these nodules and reduce diagnostic surgery. Comparative effectiveness across test generations had not been systematically pooled.

METHODS

PRISMA-guided systematic review and meta-analysis of 132 studies encompassing 66,448 total thyroid nodules, of which 30,292 (45.6%) were indeterminate (Bethesda III/IV). Primary outcomes: surgical resection rates and ROM among resected nodules. Secondary: comparison of first-gen vs. second-gen molecular platform outcomes. NIFTP reclassification impact was evaluated as a subanalysis.

RESULTS

Molecular testing significantly reduced surgical rates vs. conventional management (OR 2.26 higher surgical rate without testing, 95% CI 1.55–3.29, p<0.001). Second-gen platforms showed lower surgical rates and higher ROM than first-gen (OR=1.71, CI 1.12–2.60, p=0.013), indicating more precise risk stratification. ROM was lower in conventionally managed nodules (OR 0.594, p=0.017), explained by inclusion of benign nodules operated for non-oncologic reasons. NIFTP reclassification attenuated between-platform malignancy rate differences.

LIMITATIONS

High heterogeneity in study populations, molecular platforms, and time periods (technology evolution mid-study); potential publication bias toward positive studies; NIFTP reclassification not uniformly applied across studies; predominantly retrospective, single-institution cohorts; molecular platforms continue to evolve.

CLINICAL TAKEAWAY

Second-generation molecular testing (Afirma GSC, ThyroSeq v3) reduces unnecessary thyroidectomies for Bethesda III/IV thyroid nodules without sacrificing detection of clinically significant malignancy. H&N surgeons and endocrinologists should incorporate second-gen molecular testing into the decision pathway for indeterminate nodules, and should favor second-gen over first-gen platforms when both are available. Patients with 'benign' molecular results can be managed with surveillance rather than diagnostic surgery.

Why score 4: Largest meta-analysis to date (132 studies, 66,448 nodules) demonstrating second-generation molecular tests significantly reduce unnecessary thyroidectomy in Bethesda III/IV nodules; directly informs surgical counseling and preoperative workup for thyroid cases.

Systematic Review & Meta-Analysisn=358

Mitomycin C in the Endoscopic Treatment of Airway Stenosis: A Systematic Review and a Meta-Analysis

Russo E et al. (IRCCS Humanitas Research Hospital, Milan)

BACKGROUND

Subglottic and tracheal stenosis (SGS/TS) — from idiopathic, autoimmune, iatrogenic, or post-intubation causes — has a high endoscopic dilation recurrence rate. Mitomycin C (MMC), a DNA-alkylating antiproliferative agent, has been applied topically at the time of dilation to inhibit fibroblast proliferation and reduce re-stenosis. Evidence supporting this practice has been heterogeneous and primarily from small observational series.

METHODS

PRISMA-guided systematic review searching PubMed/MEDLINE, Cochrane Library, Scopus, Embase, and Google Scholar. PICOS criteria defined inclusion. Single-arm meta-analysis calculated pooled rates for stenosis resolution, need for multiple endoscopic procedures, and need for open surgery. Median intervention-free interval was also reported.

RESULTS

358 patients included (median age 48, median follow-up 25.2 months). Cumulative stenosis resolution rate: 76.4% (95% CI 59.7–89.6%). Rate requiring multiple endoscopic procedures: 52.3% (95% CI 32–72%). Rate requiring alternative open surgery: 4.1% (95% CI 0.4–11.5%). Median intervention-free interval: 366 days (95% CI 270–696). Wide confidence intervals reflect high study heterogeneity.

LIMITATIONS

Single-arm analysis (no MMC vs. no-MMC comparator groups within same study); very wide confidence intervals indicate substantial heterogeneity; heterogeneous MMC concentration (0.1–1.0 mg/mL) and application times across studies; mixed stenosis etiologies confound interpretation; variable follow-up duration; publication bias likely toward positive reports.

CLINICAL TAKEAWAY

Adjuvant MMC achieves approximately 76% stenosis resolution in endoscopic airway stenosis treatment, but over half of patients require multiple procedures and confidence intervals are very wide. Current evidence is insufficient to establish MMC superiority over dilation alone. The surgery team may consider MMC in select patients (idiopathic SGS, fibrotic/non-inflammatory etiology) but should counsel about the high rate of repeat procedures and the absence of rigorous comparative data. A prospective RCT is needed before routine use can be recommended.

Why score 5: First pooled analysis quantifying MMC efficacy for endoscopic airway stenosis treatment; the 76% resolution rate with wide CIs exposes the evidence gap and directly informs pre-operative counseling and decision-making for the airway surgeon.

Early Antibiotic Discontinuation for Children With a Positive Rapid Respiratory Viral Test Hospitalized for Suspected Bacterial Tracheostomy-Associated Infections

Multicenter cohort of 365 tracheostomy-dependent children (542 hospitalizations, 6 centers) found that only 28% had early antibiotic discontinuation despite a positive respiratory viral PCR. For pediatric aerodigestive and airway teams, this supports a stewardship approach that incorporates rapid viral testing into antibiotic decision-making for this vulnerable population.

Autofluorescence Can Aid in Differentiating Normal From Pathologic Parathyroid Glands During Parathyroidectomy

Prospective study of 72 patients undergoing curative parathyroidectomy found that a near-infrared autofluorescence index (AFI = max/min ratio) significantly differentiated pathologic from normal parathyroid glands; AFI ≥ 2.4 for single adenomas and ≥ 2.5 for 4-gland hyperplasia identified pathologic tissue with AUC of 0.90 and 0.86, respectively. This intraoperative tool may reduce uncertainty when distinguishing hyperfunctional from normal glands, particularly relevant for surgeons managing challenging parathyroid anatomy.
