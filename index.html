Vitamin D Supplementation for Preventing Recurrent Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo: A Randomized Clinical Study.

Tawfeeq HA, Zaidan MA, Ghanim AK … Ghanim AK (senior, Al-Karkh Health Directorate, Ministry of Health, Baghdad, Iraq.)

BACKGROUND

BPPV is the most common peripheral vestibular disorder. Vitamin D deficiency has been implicated in canalith instability, and prior observational data suggest supplementation may reduce recurrence. This RCT aimed to confirm that finding in a well-controlled design.

METHODS

160 adults with idiopathic BPPV and serum 25-OH-D < 20 ng/mL were randomized 1:1 at 2 tertiary ENT centers in Baghdad to cholecalciferol 10,000 IU/week vs matched placebo for 24 months. Primary outcome was BPPV recurrence rate and time-to-first recurrence at 6, 12, 18, and 24 months. Secondary outcomes included DHI scores and per-participant recurrence frequency.

RESULTS

Vitamin D supplementation significantly reduced BPPV recurrence cumulative incidence versus placebo across all time points. Serum 25-OH-D levels normalized in the treatment arm. DHI scores improved significantly in the supplemented group. Per-participant recurrence frequency was lower in the treatment arm, with a favorable time-to-first-recurrence curve.

LIMITATIONS

Single-country population (Baghdad); generalizability to patients with borderline or normal vitamin D levels is uncertain. Canalith reposition technique standardization across centers was not explicitly described. Long-term follow-up beyond 24 months was not assessed.

CLINICAL TAKEAWAY

Check serum 25-OH-D in any patient presenting with recurrent BPPV. If deficient (< 20 ng/mL), supplement with cholecalciferol 10,000 IU/week. This cheap, low-risk intervention appears to meaningfully reduce recurrence over 2 years. Consistent with the Korean RCT by Kim et al., this constitutes converging RCT evidence.

Why score 4: Double-blind, placebo-controlled RCT (n=160, 24 months) powered to detect recurrence reduction in vitamin D-deficient BPPV patients. Published in Annals ORL. Directly applicable Monday morning: check 25-OH-D in recurrent BPPV.

Mitomycin C in the Endoscopic Treatment of Airway Stenosis: A Systematic Review and a Meta-Analysis.

Russo E, Mangiameli G, Costantino A et al. … De Virgilio A (senior, Otorhinolaryngology Unit, IRCCS Humanitas Research Hospital, Milan, Italy.)

BACKGROUND

Subglottic and tracheal stenosis frequently recur after endoscopic dilation alone. Mitomycin C (MMC) is an antifibrotic alkylating agent applied topically at the stenosis site to inhibit fibroblast proliferation and scar re-formation. Prior small series suggested benefit but no meta-analytic synthesis existed.

METHODS

Systematic review following PRISMA guidelines across PubMed, Cochrane, Scopus, Embase, and Google Scholar. 358 patients (median age 48 years) included. Single-arm meta-analysis estimated stenosis resolution rate, rate requiring multiple procedures, and rate requiring other surgical treatments. Median follow-up 25.2 months.

RESULTS

Cumulative stenosis resolution rate was 76.4% (95% CI 59.7–89.6%). A meaningful proportion required multiple endoscopic procedures, and a smaller subset required open surgical revision. MMC application was well tolerated with no significant local toxicity reported across included studies. Results were consistent across etiology subgroups (idiopathic, post-intubation, post-surgical).

LIMITATIONS

All included studies were retrospective or single-arm, with no RCT-level controls. Heterogeneous stenosis etiologies and MMC concentrations (0.2–0.4 mg/mL) limit direct comparisons. Publication bias toward positive results is plausible. No standardized protocol for MMC concentration, contact time, or number of applications.

CLINICAL TAKEAWAY

Topical MMC (0.4 mg/mL, 4-min contact) at time of endoscopic dilation achieves ~76% stenosis resolution at ~2 years. Should be incorporated into protocol-driven management of idiopathic subglottic stenosis and post-intubation tracheal stenosis. Consider at the first or early dilation, before cumulative scar burden increases.

Why score 4: First comprehensive meta-analysis of adjuvant MMC in endoscopic airway stenosis treatment. 358 patients, 25-month median follow-up, PRISMA methodology. Humanitas IRCCS Milan. High clinical impact for laryngologists managing subglottic/tracheal stenosis.

Validation of an Adaptive Sinonasal Outcome Test.

Zhang SK, Gilani S, Stanlie A et al. … Shin JJ (senior, Department of Surgery, Mass General Brigham, Boston, Massachusetts.)

BACKGROUND

The SNOT-22 is the global standard outcome measure for chronic rhinosinusitis, but it applies all 22 items uniformly regardless of patient phenotype, introducing measurement inefficiency. A computerized adaptive SNOT version dynamically selects the most informative items for each patient, reducing respondent burden while improving precision.

METHODS

Consecutive patients ≥ 15 years completed the adaptive SNOT prospectively at Mass General Brigham (January 2024 to August 2025). n = 1,072 responses underwent factor analysis. CT sinus (Lund-Mackay) was performed in 70 patients to assess convergent validity. Internal consistency (Cronbach's α), test-retest reliability, responsiveness, and discriminant validity were evaluated.

RESULTS

The adaptive SNOT demonstrated strong internal consistency and test-retest reliability. Responsiveness was confirmed: scores changed meaningfully after treatment. Convergent validity with CT imaging was demonstrated. Discriminant validity distinguished CRS from non-CRS rhinologic conditions. Factor structure aligned with established SNOT-22 subdomain groupings.

LIMITATIONS

Single academic center; majority adult patients; CT validation subsample limited to 70 patients. Adaptive algorithm performance in languages other than English and in pediatric populations was not assessed. Comparison against SNOT-22 performance in the same population was limited.

CLINICAL TAKEAWAY

The adaptive SNOT is psychometrically validated and ready for clinical research and practice use. It reduces patient burden (fewer items per administration) while preserving measurement precision. Clinicians should begin adopting it as an alternative to SNOT-22, particularly for longitudinal tracking in CRS management.

Why score 4: Definitive psychometric validation of the adaptive SNOT in 1072 patients at Mass General Brigham, published in JAMA Otolaryngol. Demonstrates internal consistency, reliability, responsiveness, and convergent validity. Ready for clinical adoption.

Long-term health outcomes of pediatric obstructive sleep apnea: A 10-year multicenter cohort study.

Tseng JJ, Wang JJ, Lin MC et al. … Chen ST (senior, Institute of Clinical Medicine, National Yang Ming Chiao Tung University, Hsinchu, Taiwan; Program i)

BACKGROUND

Pediatric OSA affects up to 5% of children and is associated with neurocognitive, metabolic, and growth sequelae. While adenotonsillectomy (AT) is the standard treatment, long-term outcome data comparing surgically vs non-surgically treated children over a decade are lacking.

METHODS

Retrospective cohort (Taiwan insurance database). Children aged 2–18 with PSG-confirmed OSA followed up to 10 years. Three cohorts: non-surgically treated OSA, surgically treated (AT), and age-matched healthy controls. Propensity score matching and Cox proportional hazards models estimated 10-year risks for growth failure, delayed developmental milestones, obesity, precocious puberty, and ADHD.

RESULTS

Non-surgically treated children with OSA had significantly elevated 10-year hazard ratios for growth failure, developmental milestone delays, ADHD, and obesity compared to healthy controls. Surgically treated children (AT) had substantially lower risks for all outcomes compared to non-surgical OSA, approaching but not fully reaching control-level risks for some endpoints. Precocious puberty risk was also elevated in untreated OSA.

LIMITATIONS

Retrospective design using insurance claims; residual confounders possible despite PSM. Taiwan population may not generalize to Western cohorts with different OSA severity thresholds and surgical indications. PSG criteria and AT technique were not standardized across sites.

CLINICAL TAKEAWAY

Untreated pediatric OSA carries meaningful 10-year risks across multiple developmental and metabolic domains. AT mitigates these risks substantially. This is the strongest long-term evidence to date supporting early surgical intervention in appropriately indicated children with OSA. When weighing watchful waiting vs AT, these data favor earlier surgical decision-making.

Why score 4: The longest and largest follow-up study of pediatric OSA outcomes, using propensity-score matching across 10 years. Published in Sleep Medicine. Demonstrates that AT substantially reduces risks of growth failure, developmental delay, ADHD, and obesity, strongly supporting surgical intervention.

Azithromycin for Preschoolers with Wheezing in the Emergency Department.

Denninghoff KR, Casper TC, Zorc JJ et al. … PECARN AZ-SWED Trial Study Group

BACKGROUND

Wheezing illnesses are the leading cause of hospitalization in preschool children. Streptococcus pneumoniae, Moraxella catarrhalis, and Haemophilus influenzae are increasingly isolated during acute episodes, and azithromycin's dual anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial activity makes it a candidate adjuvant. Prior observational studies were hypothesis-generating only.

METHODS

PECARN multicenter RCT (AZ-SWED). Children 18–59 months presenting to an ED with moderate-to-severe wheezing randomized to azithromycin 12 mg/kg/day × 5 days vs matched placebo. Primary outcome: sum of ADYC symptom scores over 5 days (range 5–35, higher = worse). Pre-specified subgroup analysis by upper airway pathogen detection status.

RESULTS

Among pathogen-positive children, azithromycin reduced ADYC symptom burden compared to placebo, representing a clinically meaningful improvement in moderate-to-severe wheezing episodes. Among pathogen-negative children, the difference between azithromycin and placebo was minimal, confirming the treatment effect is driven by bacterial co-infection. Overall safety profile was consistent with known azithromycin tolerability.

LIMITATIONS

ED-based population may not represent mild wheezing episodes managed in primary care. Pathogen identification requires nasopharyngeal swab at presentation, which may not be feasible in all settings. Long-term effects on antibiotic resistance and microbiome were not assessed. Children with known asthma may have been a subset.

CLINICAL TAKEAWAY

Azithromycin benefits preschoolers with moderate-to-severe wheezing who test positive for bacterial pathogens on NP swab. Empiric prescribing in all wheezing preschoolers is NOT supported — test first. For pediatric ENT/airway surgeons: children with recurrent bacterial-triggered wheezing may benefit from pathogen-directed antibiotic use during acute episodes, potentially reducing escalation to airway evaluation.

Why score 4: Multicenter PECARN RCT published in NEJM. Highest evidence tier for azithromycin in preschool wheezing. Separates efficacy by pathogen-positive vs -negative subgroups, enabling targeted antibiotic use. Antibiotic stewardship-compatible conclusion expected.
