Efficacy of Vestibular Rehabilitation Therapy on Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo After Repositioning: Improvement of Vestibular Symptoms and Vertigo Disability

Yang CH, Zhang L, Wang X … Yang CH (senior, Otolaryngology, Tianjin Fourth Central Hospital, Tianjin, China)

BACKGROUND

Canalith repositioning maneuvers resolve the canal dysfunction underlying BPPV, but a substantial proportion of patients retain residual dizziness and vestibular disability (DHI ≥20) that repositioning alone does not address. Vestibular rehabilitation therapy (VRT) is a candidate adjunct, yet randomized evidence comparing outcomes by canal subtype has been sparse.

METHODS

Prospective, stratified RCT enrolling 207 BPPV patients with residual symptoms (DHI ≥20) after standard repositioning (modified Epley for pc-BPPV; Gufoni/Barbecue for hc-BPPV). VRT group received vestibulo-ocular reflex training, habituation exercises, and pharmacotherapy (betahistine + ginkgolide) for 28 days; control group received pharmacotherapy alone. Outcomes—DHI, VAS, balance compliance, dynamic visual acuity—were assessed at day 14 and 28.

RESULTS

VRT group had significantly lower 28-day DHI (18 vs 28, p<0.001), lower VAS ratings (1.60 ± 1.40 vs 3.46 ± 1.45, p<0.001), better balance compliance (88.7% vs 61.8%), and greater visual acuity improvement (3 vs 1 Snellen line). Subtype analysis: pc-BPPV showed faster early symptom relief with VRT; hc-BPPV showed superior long-term visual acuity gains.

LIMITATIONS

Single-center Chinese study; VRT was bundled with pharmacotherapy so the isolated exercise effect cannot be cleanly separated; blinding to exercise allocation is not feasible; external validity to practices without pharmacotherapy-plus-VRT protocols may vary.

CLINICAL TAKEAWAY

For BPPV patients with residual dizziness after successful repositioning (DHI ≥20), structured VRT produces meaningful, statistically significant reductions in vestibular disability and symptom burden. Canal-subtype-tailored protocols—emphasizing early symptom relief in pc-BPPV and long-term visual acuity gains in hc-BPPV—should be integrated into routine post-repositioning care.

Why score 4: Prospective stratified RCT (n=207) published in Otology & Neurotology (flagship journal). Addresses a high-prevalence clinical problem—residual post-repositioning symptoms—with directly actionable subtype-specific results. Effect sizes are clinically meaningful (DHI 18 vs 28, 3 vs 1 Snellen line).

Mupirocin Nasal Irrigation Following Endoscopic Sinus Surgery: A Prospective, Double-Blinded, Placebo-Controlled Trial

Al-Bar MH, Alqutub A, Ruiz JW … Casiano RR (senior, Department of Otolaryngology-Head and Neck Surgery, University of Miami Miller School of Medicine, Miami, Florida, USA)

BACKGROUND

Saline nasal irrigation is the post-ESS standard of care, but the benefit of adding topical mupirocin—a bacteriostatic antibiotic—during the early postoperative inflammatory window remains uncertain. Bacterial colonization may drive early mucosal complications such as edema, adhesions, and antibiotic escalation.

METHODS

68 adult CRS patients undergoing ESS were enrolled and randomized; 56 completed follow-up. Treatment: mupirocin 0.05% nasal irrigation vs. placebo saline irrigation twice daily for 21 days. Outcomes: SNOT-22 and VAS (patient-reported), endoscopic scoring (edema, polyp formation, crusting, granulation, purulence), sinus culture results, and systemic antibiotic requirement.

RESULTS

Patient-reported QoL (SNOT-22, VAS) improved significantly from baseline in both groups with no between-group difference. Mupirocin produced significantly lower rates of endoscopic mucosal edema and polyp formation at 1 month, more frequent negative postoperative cultures, and reduced systemic antibiotic prescriptions. No difference in crusting, granulation, purulence, steroid use, or pain medication.

LIMITATIONS

Small completers (n=56); single academic center; 21-day treatment window limits insight into longer-term mucosal effects; patients with known methicillin resistance were not analyzed separately; antibiotic culture sensitivity profiles not reported.

CLINICAL TAKEAWAY

Mupirocin nasal irrigation does not improve patient-reported symptoms over saline alone, but does reduce early endoscopic inflammatory markers (edema, polyp reformation) and the rate of positive sinus cultures and systemic antibiotic use. A pragmatic role exists for mupirocin irrigation in patients at high risk for early postoperative bacterial colonization or recurrent polyp formation.

Why score 4: Prospective DBPC RCT with a clinically critical question (topical antibiotic post-ESS). Although patient-reported outcomes were equivalent, the objective endoscopic and microbiological benefits justify practice consideration. Senior author (Casiano RR, U Miami) is a recognized rhinology figure. Moderate sample size (n=56) is appropriate for an early phase answer.

Palate Repair Timing Under Age 15 Months and Early Speech Outcomes

Wren Y, Roulstone S, Persson M … Wren Y (senior, Bristol Dental School, University of Bristol, Bristol, UK)

BACKGROUND

Early primary palatoplasty (before age 12–15 months) is consensus best practice for optimizing velopharyngeal competence and speech. Whether small timing variations within the already-early <15-month window meaningfully affect passive cleft speech characteristics (CSCs)—which can persist despite early repair—remains debated among cleft teams.

METHODS

Longitudinal cohort from the UK Cleft Collective (2013–ongoing, all UK regional cleft centers). Children with cleft palate (with or without cleft lip) whose palates were repaired before 15 months were assessed for passive CSCs (nasal emission, hypernasality) at 18–24 months (n=157, mean repair age 9.6 months) and 36 months (n=174, mean repair age 9.7 months). Logistic regression adjusted for cleft type and other covariates.

RESULTS

No significant association between repair timing and passive CSCs at 18–24 months. At 36 months, each additional month of delay was independently associated with increased odds of passive CSCs (OR 1.24). Passive CSC prevalence: 18.5% at 18–24 months; 16.7% at 36 months.

LIMITATIONS

Observational cohort (no RCT); UK cleft center practice norms shape the timing distribution; passive CSCs are one speech measure and do not capture active articulation errors; long-term speech outcomes beyond age 3 not yet available; family and surgeon-level factors in timing not fully adjusted.

CLINICAL TAKEAWAY

Even within the established early window (<15 months), each month of additional delay in palatoplasty carries a ~24% increase in odds of passive cleft speech characteristics at 36 months. Cleft teams should aim for the earliest surgically safe repair, not simply meeting the 15-month cutoff. This evidence should prompt multidisciplinary discussion when scheduling delays are anticipated.

Why score 4: JAMA Otolaryngology (top specialty journal), large national UK cohort with prospective design and rigorously ascertained speech outcomes. Directly practice-relevant for every cleft team debating optimal timing within the early-repair paradigm. OR of 1.24 per month has clear clinical meaning.

Efficacy and Safety of Atomoxetine Combination Therapy for Obstructive Sleep Apnea: A Meta-Analysis of Randomized Placebo-Controlled Trials

Wang C, Liu H, Zhang X … Wang C (senior, Department of Sleep Medicine, The Second Affiliated Hospital of Henan Medical University (Henan Mental Hospital), Xinxiang, China)

BACKGROUND

Significant unmet need exists for pharmacotherapy in OSA. Atomoxetine—a norepinephrine reuptake inhibitor that increases upper airway dilator muscle tone—has been studied in combination regimens (e.g., atomoxetine + oxybutynin) as an accessible, non-PAP alternative for patients who cannot tolerate or refuse CPAP and who may not be surgical candidates.

METHODS

Systematic search of PubMed, EMBASE, Cochrane, Web of Science, and PsycINFO identified 13 RCTs (528 patients) comparing atomoxetine combination therapy to placebo. Primary efficacy outcome: change in Apnea-Hypopnea Index (AHI). Secondary outcomes: oxygen saturation metrics, adverse events. Random-effects meta-analysis; heterogeneity assessed by I².

RESULTS

Atomoxetine combination significantly reduced AHI (MD −5.83, 95% CI −9.05 to −2.62; p=0.0004; I²=20%), improved lowest oxygen saturation (MD +2.91; p=0.002), and reduced oxygen desaturation index (MD −5.40; p=0.0003). Adverse events were significantly more frequent (RR 1.65, 95% CI 1.28–2.13); dry mouth occurred in 29.2% vs. 9.5% and urination difficulty in 10.7% vs. 0.8%.

LIMITATIONS

13 RCTs totaling only 528 patients; AHI reduction (~6 events/hr) is modest in absolute terms and falls short of usual treatment thresholds; no head-to-head comparison against CPAP or surgical alternatives; adverse event rates at the 10%+ level for urinary symptoms may limit acceptance; trials mostly short-term.

CLINICAL TAKEAWAY

Atomoxetine-based combination pharmacotherapy achieves a statistically significant, low-heterogeneity AHI reduction of ~6 events/hr with improvements in oxygenation metrics. This positions it as an alternative or bridge therapy for CPAP-intolerant patients or as a pre-surgical optimization strategy. Patient counseling must address the substantial rates of dry mouth (29%) and urinary difficulty (11%), particularly in older men.

Why score 4: Meta-analysis of 13 RCTs (highest evidence tier) with low heterogeneity (I²=20%) addressing a genuine clinical unmet need—pharmacotherapy for OSA. AHI reduction is modest but consistent; adverse event quantification is clinically actionable. Sleep surgeons need to know this data before counseling patients.

Efficacy and Safety of Toripalimab in Combination with Cetuximab in Patients with Recurrent or Metastatic HNSCC: A Phase 1b/2 Study

This multicenter Phase 1b/2 trial (n=88) evaluated anti-PD-1 toripalimab plus cetuximab in platinum-refractory (Cohort A, n=45) and PD-L1-positive untreated (Cohort B, n=43) recurrent/metastatic HNSCC. Cohort A achieved an ORR of 60%, mPFS 9.9 months, and mOS 14.1 months—results that meaningfully exceed historical cetuximab monotherapy benchmarks. Cohort B showed 44.2% ORR and 18.1-month median OS. Grade ≥3 adverse events occurred in 51–53% across cohorts. These data position the combination as a promising strategy for PD-L1-selected or platinum-refractory HNSCC and support ongoing Phase 3 validation.

Rotational Chair vs. Manual Maneuvers for Non-Complex BPPV: A Prospective Randomized Study

This prospective randomized trial (n=89 evaluable) compared the TRV rotational chair to standard manual Epley/Gufoni maneuvers for uncomplicated BPPV over 12 months. No significant difference was found in maneuvers required for resolution (TRV 2.25 vs manual 1.82; p=0.19), 12-month recurrence rates, or DHI/VAS quality-of-life scores. Both approaches produced comparable resolution rates, and the TRV chair conferred no measurable clinical advantage. Multivariate analysis identified known BPPV recurrence risk factors. For otology practices evaluating whether to invest in specialized rotational chairs, this study argues against acquisition on the basis of superior clinical outcomes.

Performance Results and Timing of Cochlear Implantation in Patients With DFNA9 (p.Pro51Ser)

Radboud University Medical Center compared 56 DFNA9 (COCH gene, p.Pro51Ser) cochlear implant recipients to matched controls without hereditary HL at 6 weeks, 1 year, and 5 years. DFNA9 patients achieved comparable speech perception and quality-of-life outcomes (Nijmegen CI Questionnaire, Glasgow Benefit Inventory) to matched postlingual deaf controls. This systematic review and meta-analysis of randomized trials evaluated chitosan-based nasal packing and gels after ESS versus no treatment or standard dressings. Radicality and Revision Surgery in Vestibular Schwannoma Treatment: Balancing Optimal Outcomes with Unavoidable Necessity

This high-volume retrospective series from Charles University (1998–2025) analyzed revision VS microsurgery outcomes after failed prior microsurgery and/or stereotactic irradiation, with facial nerve function (House-Brackmann grade) as the primary endpoint. Tumor control was achievable in the majority of cases, though facial nerve outcomes were inferior to those expected in primary surgery and improved significantly with subtotal rather than total resection intent. High-volume tertiary center referral was the dominant predictor of outcome. These data should inform consent discussions for patients who have failed initial VS treatment.

3 Case-ControlLaryngology
Idiopathic Subglottic Stenosis in Pregnancy: A Case-Control Study of Management and Maternal Safety

This Johns Hopkins retrospective case-control study (n=33; 23 pregnant vs. 10 age-matched never-pregnant iSGS controls) characterized clinical course and management of iSGS during pregnancy. Approximately one-third of pregnant patients required operative dilation during pregnancy, and inter-dilation intervals and stenosis severity were comparable to non-pregnant controls, suggesting pregnancy does not dramatically accelerate stenosis progression. Variables predictive of requiring in-pregnancy intervention were identified, enabling preconception counseling. Laryngologists managing reproductive-age women with iSGS should counsel proactively before and early in pregnancy to establish surveillance and operative plans.

Positive Airway Pressure-Associated Epiglottis-Related Obstruction in Adult Men with Obstructive Sleep Apnea

Kezirian EJ and colleagues at UCLA used drug-induced sleep endoscopy (DISE) with concurrent airflow/pressure measurement to characterize PAP-associated epiglottis obstruction in OSA—a mechanism underlying PAP failure in a subset of patients. Cases with epiglottis obstruction showed measurably reduced ventilation and inspiratory airflow compared to matched controls without epiglottis involvement, both before and during PAP administration. This study provides the first objective physiologic quantification of PAP-related epiglottis collapse, explaining the clinical phenotype of patients who tolerate PAP titration but report continued symptomatic obstruction. Identification of this pattern during DISE should guide targeted intervention (epiglottoplasty, positional therapy, or oral appliance) rather than further PAP pressure escalation. This work has direct clinical utility for sleep surgeons evaluating PAP-intolerant or PAP-partial-response patients.

Impact of Tongue Tip Reconstruction on Articulatory Performance After Major Tongue Resections: Evidence From a Multicentric Study

This retrospective multicentric study across four tertiary referral centers (n=57, 2007–2023) examined whether tongue tip reconstruction following hemiglossectomy or extended glossectomy for oral squamous cell carcinoma improves articulatory performance and self-perceived speech. This 10-year retrospective analysis from the University of Minnesota quantified hearing preservation rates and predictors in adult CI recipients using soft surgery techniques. A Novel Technique for Safe Intraoperative Perilymph Sampling in Humans

O'Leary SJ and colleagues at the University of Melbourne developed and validated a novel perilymph sampling methodology using calibrated nylon epidural catheters during cochlear implantation, enabling reliable biomarker collection without significant cochlear trauma. Diagnostic Performance of Contemporary Large Language Models on Free-Text Histopathologic Descriptions in Oral and Maxillofacial Pathology

This diagnostic accuracy study benchmarked ChatGPT-5.0, Gemini-2.5-Pro, and Claude-Opus-4.1 on 155 de-identified oral and maxillofacial pathology cases using free-text histopathologic descriptions and a zero-shot prompt. All three models achieved moderate-to-good accuracy, with inter-model agreement varying by diagnostic category and case complexity. The findings illustrate both the promise and the current ceiling of LLM-assisted pathology in head and neck contexts. While not yet ready for independent clinical use, LLMs may serve as a second-opinion tool in training and low-resource settings.
