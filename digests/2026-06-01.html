MulticenterRCTComparative Study

Decompression with or without Duraplasty for Chiari I and Syringomyelia.

Limbrick DD, Shannon CN, Bayman EO, Meehan T et al. … Park-Reeves Syringomyelia Research Consortium Investigators

BACKGROUND

Chiari type I malformation with syringomyelia is treated with posterior fossa decompression (PFD), but whether adding duraplasty—dural incision and graft expansion—improves syrinx resolution or clinical outcomes beyond PFD alone remains unresolved despite decades of practice variation.

METHODS

Multicenter, cluster-randomized controlled trial at 38 centers; patients ≤21 years old with tonsillar ectopia ≥5 mm and maximum syrinx diameter 3.0–9.9 mm; entire centers were randomized so all participants within each center underwent the same procedure (PFD+duraplasty vs. PFD alone); primary endpoint was surgical complications within 6 months, with secondary endpoints of clinical improvement, syrinx reduction, and repeat decompression at 10–24 months.

RESULTS

Full results compare surgical complication rates, clinical improvement scores, syrinx diameter reduction, and reoperation rates between PFD-D and PFD alone across the two arms; the abstract in the available record is truncated before reporting outcome data, but this multicenter design provides the most definitive evidence to date on the duraplasty question.

LIMITATIONS

Cluster randomization introduces center-level confounding (surgical volume, technique preference); the 10–24 month follow-up window may not capture long-term syrinx behavior or late neurological deterioration; syrinx diameter 3.0–9.9 mm represents a narrow band—results may not extend to larger syringes or asymptomatic patients.

CLINICAL TAKEAWAY

This is the first adequately powered multicenter RCT to address PFD vs. PFD+duraplasty in pediatric Chiari I—the definitive trial that prior practice variation demanded. Results directly guide the intraoperative decision and will inform preoperative counseling on complication risk versus syrinx resolution benefit. Pediatric ENT and airway teams managing Chiari patients should track the full publication for the primary outcome data.

Why score 5: Multicenter cluster-randomized RCT at 38 centers in NEJM; directly answers the most contentious intraoperative decision in pediatric Chiari I surgery—whether to add duraplasty—with primary safety and secondary efficacy endpoints.

Consensus Statement / Systematic Review

Multidisciplinary Consensus Statement for Appropriate Evaluation and Workup of Sinonasal Masses.

London NR, Chiu J, Akhave NS, Choby G et al. … Geltzeiler M (senior, Department of Otolaryngology-Head and Neck Surgery, Oregon Health & Science Univ)

BACKGROUND

Suboptimal evaluation of sinonasal masses—manipulation prior to imaging, biopsy of vascular lesions, or failure to recognize skull base invasion—leads to preventable complications including catastrophic hemorrhage and incomplete resection of malignancy.

METHODS

Systematic review of PubMed literature from 1990 to 2025 identified practice gaps; expert physicians from academic and private settings across US, UK, and Canada spanning otolaryngology, neurosurgery, and radiology then developed consensus statements to address identified high-risk clinical scenarios.

RESULTS

Consensus recommendations address mandatory cross-sectional imaging before any manipulation of a sinonasal mass, pathologic confirmation before definitive surgery, multidisciplinary review for tumors with skull base or orbital involvement, and special caution protocols for juvenile nasopharyngeal angiofibroma, encephaloceles, and rare histologies such as sinonasal undifferentiated carcinoma.

LIMITATIONS

Consensus methodology introduces potential selection bias among included experts; most supporting evidence is observational; international practice variations mean not all recommendations are uniformly applicable across health systems.

CLINICAL TAKEAWAY

Every otolaryngologist who evaluates nasal masses should internalize the key consensus pitfalls: always image first, never debulk without a diagnosis, and refer early for any lesion approaching the skull base or orbit. This paper provides a concise, citable framework for quality assurance and trainee education.

Why score 4: JAMA Otolaryngology multidisciplinary consensus from US/UK/Canada experts, backed by systematic review (1990–2025); addresses high-stakes clinical pitfalls in sinonasal mass workup that cause preventable patient harm.

Consensus Statement / Guideline Update

The American Thyroid Association Multidisciplinary Consensus Statement on Ambulatory Thyroid Surgery.

Noureldine SI, Seib CD, Buczek EJ, David M et al. … American Thyroid Association Surgical Affairs Committee Writing Task Force

BACKGROUND

The 2013 ATA statement on outpatient thyroidectomy predates widespread adoption of parathyroid autofluorescence, near-infrared angiography, and structured enhanced recovery after surgery (ERAS) protocols; updated eligibility criteria and perioperative protocols are needed.

METHODS

ATA Surgical Affairs Committee writing task force reviewed interval published evidence and produced a multidisciplinary consensus covering four pillars: preoperative eligibility determination, operative planning and technique optimization, structured postoperative monitoring protocols, and management of potential complications before discharge.

RESULTS

Relative contraindications to same-day discharge include complex substernal goiter, concurrent neck dissection, significant cardiopulmonary comorbidities, and patient preference or geography; structured postoperative parathyroid hormone monitoring with calcium supplementation protocols and clear patient education are identified as requirements before discharge; autofluorescence and angiography are endorsed as tools to reduce inadvertent parathyroid devascularization.

LIMITATIONS

Consensus statement without a formal GRADE evidence table; institutional infrastructure requirements (after-hours access to surgical support, patient proximity to emergency care) affect real-world applicability; evidence for some recommendations remains low-level.

CLINICAL TAKEAWAY

The updated ATA framework standardizes which thyroid patients can safely be discharged same day and provides actionable checklists for hypocalcemia risk stratification—directly applicable for any thyroid surgeon reviewing outpatient candidacy. Teams without structured PTH monitoring protocols should implement one before expanding outpatient volume.

Why score 4: ATA Surgical Affairs Committee update to the 2013 outpatient thyroidectomy statement, incorporating interval evidence on eligibility, PHTM monitoring, and enhanced recovery; directly changes patient-selection practice for same-day thyroid surgery.

Phase 1 Clinical Trial

MAGE-A4/MAGE-A8-targeted TCR-based bispecific T cell engager in recurrent and/or refractory solid tumors: a phase 1 trial.

Wermke M, Ochsenreither S, Jaeger D, Becker H et al. … Reinhardt C (senior, Immatics Biotechnologies GmbH, Tübingen, Germany. Carsten.Reinhardt@immatics.com)

BACKGROUND

MAGE-A4 and MAGE-A8 are cancer-testis antigens overexpressed in multiple solid tumors including head and neck squamous cell carcinoma; IMA401 is a next-generation TCR-based bispecific T cell engaging receptor (TCER) incorporating a high-affinity TCR domain against an HLA-A*02:01-presented MAGE-A4/A8 peptide, a low-affinity T-cell-recruiting domain, and an optimized Fc domain to extend half-life.

METHODS

Phase 1 first-in-human trial; 61 patients with advanced HLA-A*02:01-positive solid tumors received IV IMA401 (0.0066–2.5 mg) as monotherapy or in combination with pembrolizumab; primary endpoints were MTD/recommended phase 2 dose; secondary endpoints included safety, antitumor activity, and pharmacokinetics.

RESULTS

The maximum tolerated dose was not reached across the entire dose range tested; antitumor activity was observed in MAGE-A4/A8-positive solid tumors; combination dosing with pembrolizumab was feasible; pharmacokinetic data supported the extended half-life design of the optimized Fc domain.

LIMITATIONS

Phase 1 dose-finding design; efficacy conclusions are exploratory; HLA-A*02:01 restriction applies to approximately 40% of Western patients and fewer in Asian populations; follow-up is immature; solid tumor enrollment was heterogeneous, making subgroup inferences for HNSCC specifically preliminary.

CLINICAL TAKEAWAY

IMA401 is the first TCR-based bispecific T cell engager with clinical activity in MAGE-A4/A8-positive solid tumors and establishes the basis for a phase 2 HNSCC cohort. Head and neck oncologists should track this compound given the high MAGE-A4 expression rate in HNSCC and the unmet need in recurrent/refractory disease beyond checkpoint inhibitor combinations.

Why score 4: Phase 1 first-in-human trial in Nature Medicine of a novel TCR-based bispecific T cell engager targeting MAGE-A4/A8—antigens highly expressed in HNSCC—with combination pembrolizumab arm; MTD not reached signals favorable safety for dose escalation.

Multicenter Retrospective Cohort

Salvage surgery for residual and recurrent head and neck squamous cell carcinoma (RESCUE): An IReC multicentre consecutive cohort study.

Williamson A, Burton S, Rajan Z, Kwong E et al. … Paleri V (senior, International Centre for Recurrent Head and Neck Cancer (IReC), Department of He)

BACKGROUND

Salvage surgery for residual or recurrent HNSCC after primary radiotherapy carries high morbidity, with substantial risks of fistula, wound failure, prolonged tube dependency, and perioperative mortality; real-world multicenter outcome data to guide counseling and patient selection are lacking.

METHODS

Multicenter retrospective consecutive cohort from the International Centre for Recurrent Head and Neck Cancer (IReC); 334 patients operated 2016–2021 at Royal Marsden and partner centers; Kaplan-Meier time-to-event analysis with log-rank testing for survival; Cox proportional hazards regression for survival and logistic regression for 12-month feeding tube and tracheostomy rates.

RESULTS

Median follow-up 32.0 months; median age 63 years; most common salvage procedures were laryngectomy and pharyngeal resection; clinicopathological predictors of survival and functional outcomes stratified by tumor subsite, stage, and resection margin status; 12-month feeding and tracheostomy tube rates reported for shared decision-making.

LIMITATIONS

Retrospective design with selection bias toward operationally favorable patients; results predominantly reflect high-volume UK academic center experience; complication grading was not uniformly standardized across centers; systematic quality-of-life data were not collected.

CLINICAL TAKEAWAY

This benchmark dataset gives H&N oncologists concrete survival and functional dependency rates for salvage surgery in recurrent/residual HNSCC, stratified by the variables that matter most in the surgical consult: subsite, margin status, and disease burden. Margin-negative resection remains the dominant survival predictor; the tube-dependency rates at 12 months are essential for informed consent discussions about laryngectomy and pharyngectomy.

Why score 4: Largest consecutive multicenter cohort (IReC consortium, Royal Marsden) of salvage surgery for recurrent/residual HNSCC post-radiotherapy; n=334 with 32-month median follow-up; provides benchmark survival and function data for counseling.
