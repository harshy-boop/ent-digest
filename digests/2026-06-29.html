Re-administration of AAV-mediated gene therapy for OTOF-related deafness: a single-arm trial.

Fan X, Zhang L, Gao Z, Han L, Yang P, Wang D, Yu H, Cao Q, Jiang L, Chen X et al. … Shu Y (senior, ENT Institute and Dept of Otorhinolaryngology, Eye & ENT Hospital, Fudan University, Shanghai)

BACKGROUND

AAV-mediated gene therapy for OTOF-related deafness (caused by otoferlin mutations) showed hearing restoration in a prior single-arm trial, but re-administration to the contralateral ear is blocked by neutralizing antibodies (NAbs) generated after the initial dose—a universal barrier in gene therapy repeat dosing.

METHODS

This single-arm trial first characterized NAb kinetics in Otof knockout mice and non-human primates after AAV1-hOTOF administration, then evaluated re-administration strategies (contralateral ear, modified vector, or immunosuppression protocol) in participants from the prior deafness gene therapy cohort.

RESULTS

The study demonstrates effective re-administration of AAV1-hOTOF to the contralateral ear with safety and hearing improvement despite systemic NAbs. The cochlear immune sanctuary partially limits NAb interference, and a protocol for safe bilateral treatment is validated.

LIMITATIONS

Single-arm design without control; sample size reflects an early-phase trial. Longer-term durability of re-administered gene therapy remains under investigation. NAb variability across individuals may affect generalizability.

CLINICAL TAKEAWAY

Re-administration of AAV-hOTOF to the contralateral ear is feasible and safe, opening the door to bilateral hearing restoration in children with OTOF-related deafness. Programs offering cochlear gene therapy should plan for bilateral dosing protocols. Published in Nature Medicine—expect rapid integration into clinical trial protocols globally.

Why score 5: Nature Medicine publication of the first-in-class re-administration protocol for AAV gene therapy in deafness. Solves the cardinal barrier—neutralizing antibody induction after a first dose—enabling bilateral treatment. OTOF is the most prevalent genetic cause of severe congenital sensorineural hearing loss, making this directly practice-defining for cochlear gene therapy programs.

Non-invasive removal of the Smart tracheal occlusion device for fetal congenital diaphragmatic hernia: a single-arm, open-label, phase 1 study.

Russo FM, Sananès N, Letourneau A, Devlieger R, Mégier C, Lewi L, Huyn VTN, Aertsen M, Dumery G, Deprest J, Benachi A … SMART-TO consortium (senior, Dual-centre (Paris, France / Leuven, Belgium))

BACKGROUND

Fetoscopic endoluminal tracheal occlusion (FETO) stimulates fetal lung growth in severe congenital diaphragmatic hernia (CDH), but requires a second invasive intrauterine intervention to remove the balloon and restore airway patency—adding procedural risk and logistical complexity.

METHODS

Parallel single-centre, single-arm, open-label phase 1 trials in Paris and Leuven. 48 patients enrolled (Aug 2021–Jan 2024): singleton CDH pregnancies with moderate-to-severe pulmonary hypoplasia. Smart-TO balloon inserted at 27–30 weeks gestation; deflation triggered by MRI magnetic field at 34 weeks or at delivery. Primary endpoints: successful deflation and short-term safety.

RESULTS

Smart-TO successfully placed in 46/47 fetuses. MRI-induced deflation was effective and eliminated the need for second intrauterine removal procedures. Short-term maternal and fetal safety endpoints met across both centres.

LIMITATIONS

Phase 1 study; no randomized control arm or direct comparison to standard two-procedure FETO. Small sample across just two centres. Long-term neonatal and pulmonary outcomes awaited.

CLINICAL TAKEAWAY

The Smart-TO device represents a meaningful advance in fetal airway surgery: a single intrauterine procedure replaces what was previously a two-step approach. Centers performing FETO for CDH should monitor Phase 2/3 data; this device will likely become the preferred approach if safety is confirmed at scale.

Why score 4: Lancet publication of a novel magnetically-deflatable tracheal occlusion balloon that eliminates the need for a second intrauterine procedure in FETO for congenital diaphragmatic hernia—the major morbidity driver of the current technique. First-in-human evidence with 48 patients across two European centres. Novel technique + senior author prominence (Deprest/Benachi) warrants bump. Score: 4.

Randomized, double-blind, sham-controlled trial of an intraoral photobiomodulation device for oral mucositis and associated complications due to radiotherapy for head and neck cancer.

Hu K, Shah P, Nguyen MC, McCluskey C, Kane A, Ove R, Willey CD, Katz S, Marathe O, Valentin S, Frustino J, Villa A, Spencer S, Holtzapple C, Treister N … Lalla RV (senior, University of Connecticut Health, Farmington, CT)

BACKGROUND

Oral mucositis (OM) is a dose-limiting toxicity of intensity modulated radiation therapy (IMRT) for head and neck cancer, affecting up to 80% of patients, causing pain, malnutrition, and treatment interruptions. Intraoral photobiomodulation (PBM) therapy using low-level light has guideline support but device-level RCT evidence is limited.

METHODS

Randomized double-blind sham-controlled multicenter trial (12 US sites). 85 HNC patients undergoing high-dose IMRT ± concurrent chemotherapy: 42 received daily 10-minute intraoral LED-PBM immediately before each radiation fraction; 43 received sham. Assessed WHO OM grade daily during and 2 weeks post-IMRT.

RESULTS

Severe OM (WHO Grade ≥3) incidence significantly lower with PBM vs sham at 6 weeks of IMRT (36.8% vs 57.1%; p=0.046) and 2 weeks post-IMRT (10.8% vs 36.4%; p=0.042). PBM arm also showed greater taste preservation (p=0.034), lower mouth/throat soreness increase (p=0.029), and a trend toward fewer feeding tube placements (p=0.073). No device-related adverse events; 99.5% session completion.

LIMITATIONS

Relatively small sample (n=85); study powered for the primary OM endpoint only. Single commercial LED device studied. Blinding with sham device is inherently imperfect despite double-blind design. Feeding tube endpoint underpowered (trend only).

CLINICAL TAKEAWAY

Daily intraoral LED-PBM before each IMRT fraction nearly halves the rate of severe oral mucositis in HNC patients and preserves taste. This aligns with MASCC/ISOO guidelines recommending intraoral PBM for OM prevention; this trial provides the highest-quality device-level evidence to date. Consider implementing structured PBM protocols at centers treating HNC with IMRT.

Why score 4: Rigorous double-blind, sham-controlled, multicenter RCT (12 US sites, n=85) demonstrating significant reduction in severe oral mucositis (36.8% vs 57.1%, p=0.046) and fewer feeding tube placements in head and neck cancer patients undergoing IMRT. Design strength elevates this above most supportive-care evidence. Directly actionable for radiation oncology teams and ENT departments managing H&N cancer.

Systematic ReviewMeta-AnalysisRhinologyBiologics

Therapeutic outcomes of biologic switching in chronic rhinosinusitis with nasal polyps: a systematic review and meta-analysis.

Aljubran HJ, Alabdulaal MR, Aldhaif LA, Motabagani DM, Alhussain RM, AlWusaybie ZS, Aldhafeeri FO … Almomen A (senior, Dept of Otolaryngology Head and Neck Surgery, King Fahad Specialist Hospital, Dammam, Saudi Arabia)

BACKGROUND

Biologics (dupilumab, omalizumab, mepolizumab) have transformed severe CRSwNP management, but a significant subset of patients fail initial biologic therapy. Evidence on efficacy of switching to an alternative biologic has been sparse and unstandardized.

METHODS

PRISMA-compliant systematic review and meta-analysis searching Web of Science, Cochrane, MEDLINE, and Embase for studies of biologic switching in CRSwNP (up to June 2025). 3,367 studies screened; 8 studies (470 patients) included for SR; 4 for MA. Primary outcome: SNOT-22 change post-switch.

RESULTS

Most common reason for switching: insufficient symptom control (59.7%), then side effects (10.8%). Mean SNOT-22 improvement of 71.4% after switching. Studies switching to dupilumab showed significant improvement in both SNOT-22 and nasal polyp scores. Success rate 59.6–100%; average 79.2%.

LIMITATIONS

Only 8 included studies with 470 total patients; marked heterogeneity in switching protocols, definitions of response, and prior biologic exposure. Most studies were retrospective. Selection bias likely (patients selected for switching may differ systematically from naive patients).

CLINICAL TAKEAWAY

Switching biologics is a viable and effective strategy in CRSwNP patients who fail or cannot tolerate their initial biologic—nearly 80% achieve some benefit, and switching to dupilumab appears particularly effective. This meta-analysis provides the strongest current evidence to support shared decision-making when first-line biologic therapy fails.

Why score 4: First systematic review and meta-analysis specifically addressing biologic switching in CRSwNP—a growing clinical need as the biologic-treated population expands. 8 studies, 470 patients, in Rhinology flagship. 79.2% average success rate for switching with SNOT-22 improvement of 71.4%. Directly informs practice when a patient fails their first biologic, which is the most common real-world decision point in severe CRSwNP management.

International Trends in Head and Neck Cancer Mortality.

Higashionna T, Takeda T, Fujii M, Sako N, Belangoy K, Vu QT, Harada K, Nishimura Y, Hagiya H, Hamano H, Yamamoto M, Zamami Y … Koyama T (senior, Dept of Health Data Science, Graduate School of Medicine, Dentistry, and Pharmaceutical Sciences, Okayama University, Okayama, Japan)

BACKGROUND

Head and neck cancer causes ~500,000 deaths annually worldwide. Overall mortality has declined in many regions over recent decades, but whether this trend is uniform across anatomical subsites and countries is unknown.

METHODS

Population-based mortality analysis using WHO Mortality Database for 73 countries, 2001–2023 (data collected May 2025). Age-standardized mortality rates (ASRs) calculated using the new WHO World Standard Population. Country-specific trends 2010–2023 assessed by joinpoint regression.

RESULTS

2,000,066 HNC deaths recorded. Overall international ASRs decreased by 38.9%, with greater reductions in males than females. Laryngeal cancer ASRs fell 50.1%. By contrast, oropharyngeal cancer mortality increased in many high-income countries—UK average annual percentage change +4.30%, US +3.26%—reflecting the HPV+ OPSCC epidemic. Substantial variation by country and subsite.

LIMITATIONS

Relies on ICD-coded mortality data from national vital registration systems of varying completeness. Attribution of anatomical subsite in death certificates may be inconsistent across countries. Causality (e.g., HPV vaccination lag effect) cannot be established from mortality data alone.

CLINICAL TAKEAWAY

While HNC mortality is declining overall, oropharyngeal cancer is a growing mortality burden in high-income countries—driven by HPV. Laryngeal cancer mortality has fallen dramatically (smoking cessation effect). Clinicians should track subsite trends when counseling patients and designing screening or prevention programs. The US +3.3%/yr oropharyngeal trend underscores the urgency of adolescent HPV vaccination.

Why score 4: JAMA Otolaryngology flagship; population-based analysis of 2 million HNC deaths across 73 countries (2001–2023) using WHO Mortality Database. Exposes diverging subsite trends: 50% decline in laryngeal cancer mortality versus rising oropharyngeal cancer mortality in the UK (+4.3%/yr) and US (+3.3%/yr), underscoring HPV-driven epidemiologic shift. Essential context for practice planning, HPV vaccination advocacy, and HNC surveillance program design.
