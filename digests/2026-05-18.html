Ghanim AK et al. … Ghanim AK (senior, Al-Karkh Health Directorate, Ministry of Health, Baghdad, Iraq)

BACKGROUND

Benign paroxysmal positional vertigo (BPPV) is the most common cause of recurrent positional vertigo in ENT practice. Vitamin D deficiency has been implicated in otolith degeneration and higher BPPV recurrence rates, but prospective evidence for supplementation as secondary prevention has been limited.

METHODS

Prospective, double-blind, placebo-controlled RCT at two Baghdad tertiary ENT centers. Adults with confirmed idiopathic BPPV and serum 25-OH-D < 20 ng/mL (n=160) were randomized 1:1 to cholecalciferol 10,000 IU/week vs. matched placebo for 24 months. Primary endpoint: cumulative BPPV recurrence at 6, 12, 18, and 24 months, analyzed by full intention-to-treat with multiple imputation for missing data.

RESULTS

Cholecalciferol significantly raised 25-OH-D at all timepoints (24-month level: 24.8 ± 3.2 vs. 9.8 ± 3.6 ng/mL in placebo). BPPV recurrence rates were significantly lower in the vitamin D arm at every interval. Dizziness Handicap Inventory scores also improved more in the treatment group. No significant adverse events were observed in either arm.

LIMITATIONS

Single geographic setting (Baghdad) may limit generalizability across populations with differing baseline vitamin D status and sun exposure. Small center count (n=2). Mechanism by which vitamin D reduces BPPV recurrence remains speculative (probable otolith stabilization). Subgroup analyses by BPPV canal type were not reported.

CLINICAL TAKEAWAY

Measure 25-OH-D in BPPV patients. For those with deficiency (< 20 ng/mL), initiate cholecalciferol supplementation (10,000 IU/week or equivalent). This is low-cost, low-risk, and — based on this RCT — meaningfully cuts recurrence over 2 years. Consider incorporating vitamin D screening into BPPV follow-up protocols.

Why score 4: Double-blind, placebo-controlled RCT (n=160), 24-month follow-up, ITT analysis. Clinically actionable: weekly cholecalciferol significantly reduces BPPV recurrence in vitamin D–deficient patients — a low-cost, office-initiatable intervention for one of ENT's most common vestibular presentations.

Expert ReviewLancet FamilyThyroid Oncology

Schlumberger M … Schlumberger M (senior, Dept. Nuclear Medicine & Endocrine Oncology, Gustave Roussy, Villejuif, France)

BACKGROUND

Papillary thyroid cancer (PTC) frequently spreads to central compartment lymph nodes, but most central metastases are clinically silent and missed on preoperative ultrasound (cN0 disease). Prophylactic central neck dissection (pCND) is the only method to detect and remove these micrometastases, yet guidelines have left the decision open for clinician discretion for over a decade.

METHODS

Expert synthesis (Personal View) of the available clinical data, including prospective and retrospective studies, existing RCTs, and international guideline positions, evaluating survival, regional recurrence, and procedure-related morbidity for pCND vs. no pCND in cN0 PTC.

RESULTS

No survival benefit has been demonstrated for pCND. Some studies show modest reductions in regional recurrence rates (<10% clinically detected recurrence even without pCND), but with a concomitant increase in morbidity, primarily temporary hypoparathyroidism. Definitive RCTs in surgical oncology remain methodologically difficult.

LIMITATIONS

The evidence base is predominantly retrospective and single-center. Tumor biology factors (BRAF V600E, tumor size, extrathyroidal extension) are not yet well-integrated into risk-stratified decision frameworks. Randomized surgical trials in thyroid cancer are methodologically challenging and have rarely been completed.

CLINICAL TAKEAWAY

Routine pCND in all cN0 PTC patients is not evidence-based and adds morbidity without demonstrated survival gain. A risk-stratified approach — weighing tumor size, extrathyroidal extension, BRAF status, and institutional complication rates — is more defensible. This influential review calls for prospective molecular-guided research to finally resolve this debate.

Why score 4: Personal View by Michel Schlumberger (Gustave Roussy) — the pre-eminent figure in thyroid oncology — published in The Lancet Diabetes & Endocrinology. Directly addresses one of the most persistently debated surgical decisions in thyroid cancer: prophylactic central neck dissection in cN0 papillary thyroid cancer. Likely to influence the next revision of international guidelines.

Rana C et al. … Rana C (senior, Dept. Pathology, King George's Medical University, Lucknow, India)

BACKGROUND

Indeterminate thyroid cytology (Bethesda III/IV) accounts for 15–30% of FNA specimens and has historically prompted diagnostic lobectomy or thyroidectomy in the majority of cases, even though only 10–30% of resected indeterminate nodules prove malignant. Molecular tests (e.g., Afirma GSC, ThyroSeq v3) aim to improve preoperative risk stratification and reduce unnecessary surgery.

METHODS

PRISMA-guided systematic review and meta-analysis of 132 studies including 66,448 total nodules (30,292 indeterminate). Primary outcomes: surgical resection rate and risk of malignancy (ROM) stratified by conventional management vs. molecular testing, and by first- vs. second-generation molecular platform.

RESULTS

Molecular testing was associated with significantly lower surgical rates than conventional management (OR 2.258, 95% CI 1.55–3.29; p < 0.001). Second-generation platforms further reduced surgical rates vs. first-generation (OR 1.709; 95% CI 1.12–2.60). Malignancy detection was maintained: ROM was slightly higher in the molecular testing group, reflecting better enrichment of resected specimens for true malignancy.

LIMITATIONS

High methodological heterogeneity across 132 studies. Variable definitions of 'indeterminate' and inconsistent handling of NIFTP (non-invasive follicular thyroid neoplasm with papillary-like nuclear features), which inflated ROM in some cohorts. Most studies are from high-income countries with access to commercial testing platforms.

CLINICAL TAKEAWAY

For Bethesda III/IV thyroid nodules, molecular testing — particularly second-generation platforms — meaningfully reduces the proportion of patients sent to surgery without sacrificing detection of clinically significant malignancy. This supports reflexive molecular testing for indeterminate FNA results rather than proceeding directly to diagnostic hemithyroidectomy.

Why score 4: Largest global meta-analysis to date on molecular testing in indeterminate thyroid nodules (n=66,448 nodules, 132 studies). Demonstrates that molecular testing reduces unnecessary thyroid surgery 2.3-fold vs. conventional management, with second-generation platforms performing better than first-generation. Directly practice-changing for endocrine surgeons, thyroid cytopathologists, and ENT surgeons managing thyroid disease.

Hamming-Vrieze O et al. … Hamming-Vrieze O (senior, Dept. Radiation Oncology, Netherlands Cancer Institute, Amsterdam)

BACKGROUND

FDG-PET–guided adaptive dose redistribution (rRT) was hypothesized to improve locoregional control (LRC) in T3-4 N0-3 locally advanced HNSCC by targeting higher doses to metabolically resistant tumor subvolumes. The ARTFORCE trial previously reported non-significant 2-year results; this paper presents the definitive 5-year oncologic and toxicity data.

METHODS

Multicenter Phase III trial (221 eligible); patients randomized 1:1 to rRT (64–84 Gy in 35 fractions, PET adaptation at fraction 10) vs. conventional RT (cRT; 70 Gy in 35 fractions). Both arms received concurrent cisplatin 100 mg/m² × 3 cycles. Trial closed at 84% of planned accrual. Primary endpoint: LRC.

RESULTS

5-year LRC: HR 0.78 (95% CI 0.45–1.36; p = 0.39) — no significant difference. Acute toxicity rates were similar (p ≥ 0.10 for all). Late toxicity: higher grade ≥3 pharyngolaryngeal stenosis in the rRT arm (4% vs. 0%; p = 0.05). Hypothyroidism and fibrosis increased in both arms through 5 years. No additional subgroups benefited from rRT in exploratory analyses.

LIMITATIONS

Trial closed at 84% accrual, reducing statistical power to detect modest differences in LRC. Exploratory subgroup analysis only (oropharyngeal, N0-1 patients showed a trend favoring rRT). Single adaptation timepoint (fraction 10) may not optimize tumor response imaging. Heterogeneous histologies (larynx, pharynx, oral cavity) may dilute site-specific effects.

CLINICAL TAKEAWAY

PET-guided adaptive dose redistribution does not improve tumor control over standard 70-Gy chemoradiation in unselected locally advanced HNSCC, and carries a higher risk of severe pharyngolaryngeal stenosis. Standard concurrent chemoradiation remains the benchmark. Personalized dose-escalation strategies should be tested only in carefully selected subgroups within clinical trials.

Why score 4: Phase III multicenter RCT (NCI Amsterdam) with 5-year oncologic and toxicity outcomes in locally advanced HNSCC. Final data confirm PET-guided adaptive dose redistribution does not improve locoregional control vs. conventional chemoradiation, while adding higher rates of pharyngolaryngeal stenosis. Settles a key question in head and neck radiation oncology practice.

Shin JJ et al. … Shin JJ (senior, Dept. Surgery, Mass General Brigham, Boston, MA)

BACKGROUND

The 22-item Sino-Nasal Outcome Test (SNOT-22) is the most widely used patient-reported outcome measure for chronic rhinosinusitis (CRS), but a well-recognized limitation is its poor concordance with objective CT imaging severity. An adaptive version using item response theory — the Adaptive SNOT — was developed to address this. This study formally validates its psychometric properties.

METHODS

Validation diagnostic study of consecutive patients ≥15 years (n=1,072) at Mass General Brigham. Evaluated: factor analysis, internal consistency (Cronbach α), intrarater reliability (Cohen κ, ICC), responsiveness to CRS treatment (medical and surgical), and convergent validity with PROMIS health domains. CT sinuses performed in a 70-patient subgroup for objective disease severity comparison.

RESULTS

Adaptive SNOT clearly distinguished low vs. high CT burden (Cohen d = 0.80 for nasal domain). Intrarater reliability was substantial (Cohen κ 0.72–0.75). The instrument was responsive to treatment (pre-treatment mean 9.12 → post-treatment 2.47; Cohen d = 0.76). Convergent validity ranged from low to strong across PROMIS physical, mental, social, and global health domains.

LIMITATIONS

Single academic network (MGB); generalizability to community settings needs confirmation. CT subgroup was small (n=70). Adaptive SNOT has not been compared to SNOT-22 in a head-to-head interventional trial. Digital delivery infrastructure required for adaptive administration may not yet be universally available.

CLINICAL TAKEAWAY

The adaptive SNOT is now formally validated for CRS and allergic rhinitis assessment in clinical and research settings. Its key advantage — demonstrated in the companion paper (PMID 42133355) — is concordance with CT imaging that the SNOT-22 lacks. Practices and investigators using the SNOT-22 should consider transitioning to the adaptive version, which reduces question burden while improving objective correlation.

Why score 4: Comprehensive psychometric validation of the adaptive Sino-Nasal Outcome Test (SNOT), published in JAMA Otolaryngology from Mass General Brigham (n=1072). Demonstrates strong internal consistency, intrarater reliability, and responsiveness to treatment — and, uniquely, correlation with CT imaging findings (detailed in the companion paper, PMID 42133355). The SNOT-22 has been the CRS standard for decades; this validated adaptive successor is clinically ready.

Mayo Clinic (Jacksonville) systematic review of 36 studies / 687 patients evaluating whether postoperative ICP-lowering interventions reduce recurrent lateral skull base spontaneous CSF leak after primary repair. Pooled data from six comparative cohorts showed no significant difference in recurrence with vs. without ICP therapy (RR 1.16, 95% CI 0.14–9.76). Acetazolamide may be associated with lower recurrence in observational data; lumbar drainage has not demonstrated sustained benefit. Takeaway: routine postoperative ICP-lowering is not universally supported — individualize based on patient risk and emerging prospective data.
