Adverse Airway Events Following Head and Neck Cancer Reconstruction Without Elective Tracheostomy

Young TC, Tsai TY, Chan KC

Background

Whether to perform elective tracheostomy (ET) at the time of head and neck cancer free-flap reconstruction is one of the most common intraoperative decisions an ENT surgeon makes. Prior studies catalogued factors driving the ET decision, but the actual rate of adverse airway events (AAEs) in patients managed without ET was poorly characterised.

Methods

Systematic review + meta-analysis (PRISMA) of Embase, PubMed, Scopus, Web of Science, and Ovid MEDLINE (2009–2026). 25 studies, n = 5,987 patients not receiving elective tracheostomy. Pooled ORs used to identify AAE predictors; number-needed-to-harm framework applied to ET complication data.

Results

Pooled AAE incidence without ET: 10.3% (95% CI 6.7–15.6%). Highest-risk sites: floor-of-mouth (OR 5.96) and oropharynx (OR 2.60) vs other oral cavity subsites. Bilateral neck dissection (OR 6.36) and higher ASA class (OR 1.51) were independent predictors. ET itself was associated with increased flap complications (OR 2.15), unplanned return to OR (OR 1.73), and delayed oral feeding (+5.86 days).

Limitations

Heterogeneity across included studies in AAE definitions and patient selection. Observational study design precludes causal inference. Potential publication bias toward higher-volume centres.

Clinical Takeaway

A 1-in-10 adverse airway rate without ET demands pre-operative risk stratification, not a blanket policy. Floor-of-mouth and oropharyngeal tumours with bilateral neck dissection and poor ASA status represent the highest-risk phenotype; ET may be warranted despite its morbidity in these patients. Conversely, routine ET adds real costs and delays — use it selectively.

Why score 4: PRISMA-compliant meta-analysis of 25 studies (n=5,987) in the specialty's flagship journal. Directly informs perioperative airway planning decisions every head and neck surgeon faces. Pooled ORs are clinically actionable.

Neuromodulation for Subjective Tinnitus: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis of Randomized Trials

Kitsis D, Sideris G, Skouloudaki A, Delides A, Vlastarakos P

Background

Chronic subjective tinnitus lacks an FDA-approved pharmacological cure. Neuromodulation (transcranial electrical stimulation, repetitive TMS, bimodal somatosensory–acoustic stimulation, vagus nerve stimulation) has been tested in RCTs with inconsistent results; the landscape needed a rigorous meta-analysis.

Methods

PRISMA 2020. PubMed/MEDLINE, Web of Science, EMBASE searched January 2015 – December 2025. Included: adult RCTs ≥ 18 yr with chronic tinnitus > 3 months, validated instruments (THI, TFI, TQ). 26 RCTs (n=1,576) met criteria. Random-effects REML meta-analyses with SMDs.

Results

tDCS: non-significant pooled effect (SMD –0.36; 95% CI –0.75 to 0.02). Bimodal somatosensory–acoustic stimulation showed the most consistent and durable clinical improvements across modalities. All modalities had acceptable safety profiles with no serious adverse events.

Limitations

High between-trial heterogeneity in protocols, sham controls, and outcome instruments. Small individual trial sizes. Long-term follow-up (> 6 months) was sparse. Publication bias likely.

Clinical Takeaway

Bimodal stimulation (e.g., Lenire device) is the most evidence-backed non-invasive option with durable effect. Standard tDCS and rTMS have not convincingly separated from sham in pooled analysis. Frame patient expectations accordingly — average benefit is modest. Referral to audiology for sound therapy remains first-line.

Why score 4: 26 RCTs, n=1,576, published in the Laryngoscope. Tinnitus affects 10–15% of the population; this is the most up-to-date synthesis of non-invasive neuromodulation — a space patients increasingly ask about.

Hypoglossal Nerve Stimulation and Hypoxic Burden in Patients With Obstructive Sleep Apnea: A Secondary Analysis of the STAR Trial

Xu J, Hajipour Z, Aloia MS, de Beeck SO … Azarbarzin A (senior, Division of Sleep and Circadian Disorders, Brigham and Women's Hospital and Harvard Medical School)

Background

HGNS reduces AHI, but AHI is a poor surrogate for OSA-related cardiovascular and neurocognitive risk. Hypoxic burden (HB — cumulative oxygen desaturation area under the curve) may better capture that risk. Whether HGNS reduces HB, and whether HB reduction translates to symptom benefit, was unknown.

Methods

Secondary analysis of the STAR trial (NCT01161420), a multicenter non-randomized trial with a randomized withdrawal component. n=108 adults with moderate-to-severe OSA intolerant of PAP. Polysomnography at baseline and 12 months. Primary outcome: HB change; secondary: correlation of HB with Epworth Sleepiness Scale.

Results

Baseline median HB 63.4 %min/h. After 12 months of HGNS, median HB fell 76.6% (IQR 33.1–90.7%). ESS improvement correlated with HB reduction (β=1.25; 95% CI 0.02–2.48) but not with AHI, Arousal Index, or oxygen nadir. Critically, 9/18 (50%) of Sher-20 AHI non-responders still showed marked HB improvement.

Limitations

Secondary analysis not powered for HB outcomes. Lack of randomised control arm for full 12-month analysis. Single-device (Inspire) results may not generalise to other HGNS platforms.

Clinical Takeaway

Consider reporting hypoxic burden alongside AHI when evaluating HGNS response. A patient who remains AHI > 20 but achieves 80% HB reduction may be deriving meaningful cardiovascular benefit. Daytime sleepiness persisting despite apparent AHI non-response deserves HB re-evaluation — and should not automatically trigger device explanation or change in therapy.

Why score 4: Flagship ENT journal (JAMA Otolaryngology), Harvard/BWH senior authorship, secondary analysis of the landmark STAR trial. Reshapes how HGNS treatment response is defined and communicated to patients.

Clinical Practice Guideline for the Evaluation and Management of Facial Nerve Palsy

Lee SA, Lee HY, Kwak MY, Kim J, Yoo MC … Lee JD (senior)

Background

Peripheral facial nerve palsy — encompassing Bell's palsy, Ramsay Hunt syndrome, and iatrogenic/traumatic palsies — is the most common mononeuropathy in ENT practice. Management varies widely; this guideline synthesises current evidence to standardise evaluation and treatment.

Methods

Multidisciplinary committee (otolaryngologists, neurologist, neurosurgeon, rehab specialist, ophthalmologist, plastic surgeon). Systematic literature review through November 30, 2024 (MEDLINE, Embase, Cochrane, KoreaMed). GRADE appraisal. 9 key clinical questions. Patient preference survey (n=51). External review by 7 independent experts.

Results

10 recommendations: House-Brackmann grading as standard (Grade A). Routine testing unnecessary for typical Bell's palsy; selective for atypical features (Grade B). Electrodiagnostic studies for prognostic evaluation in severe cases (Grade B). Oral corticosteroids strongly recommended within 72 h of onset (Grade A). Antivirals may be added per clinical judgment (Grade B). Surgical decompression in selected patients with severe degeneration (Grade B). Rehabilitation, botulinum toxin for synkinesis, and multidisciplinary care conditionally recommended (Grade B).

Limitations

Developed within the Korean healthcare system (NHI coverage and regional accessibility explicitly embedded); some recommendations may not translate 1:1 to US practice. Patient preference survey was small (n=51).

Clinical Takeaway

For acute Bell's palsy: corticosteroids within 72 hours is Grade A — non-negotiable. Antivirals are Grade B, reasonable to add especially in Ramsay Hunt. House-Brackmann remains the standard grading tool. Reserve ENoG for clinically severe cases to guide decompression decisions. Synkinesis → botulinum toxin is conditionally supported.

Why score 4: Evidence-based CPG using GRADE methodology from a multidisciplinary committee. Facial nerve palsy spans all ENT subspecialties; 10 actionable recommendations with graded evidence directly applicable to practice.

Systematic Review & Meta-Analysis

Inflammatory Myofibroblastic Tumour of Head and Neck: A Systematic Review Plus Meta-Analysis

Sharifi A, Zojaji M, Badragheh N, Jamali M … Turner MT (senior, Dept of Otolaryngology–HNS, West Virginia University)

Background

Inflammatory myofibroblastic tumour (IMT) is a rare intermediate-grade mesenchymal neoplasm disproportionately involving the head and neck. It can mimic inflammatory and malignant conditions, making diagnosis challenging and surgical planning uncertain.

Methods

PRISMA-adherent systematic review + meta-analysis. 7 databases searched through February 2026. 22 studies, n=337 patients. Primary outcomes: clinical presentation, IHC findings, treatment approach, recurrence, metastasis.

Results

Most common origin: sinonasal space (40.1%). Dominant symptoms: proptosis and facial pain/swelling. IHC: positive for vimentin and SMA; negative for desmin, S-100, ALK, CD34. Treatment: surgical resection alone sufficient for most. Recurrence rate: 21.6%. Metastasis rate: 8.8%. CT calcification absence may distinguish from other diagnoses.

Limitations

Most included studies were case reports and small series; high selection bias risk. Heterogeneous treatment protocols and follow-up durations. ALK-positivity rates could not be reliably pooled. Level of evidence inherently limited for rare tumours.

Clinical Takeaway

When encountering a sinonasal/head-and-neck mass with proptosis or facial swelling and histopathology showing spindle cells positive for vimentin and SMA, consider IMT. Surgical resection with free margins is primary treatment. Counsel patients on 21.6% recurrence and 8.8% metastasis risk; long-term surveillance is warranted even after apparently complete resection.

Why score 4: First comprehensive systematic review + meta-analysis of IMT in the head and neck in Clinical Otolaryngology. Provides pooled recurrence (21.6%) and metastasis (8.8%) rates surgeons need for counselling this rare but clinically tricky diagnosis.

Clinical Characteristics, Diagnostic Features, and Medical Treatment of Sinonasal Sarcoidosis: A Systematic Review

A systematic review (n=369 patients, 38 studies) examined sinonasal sarcoidosis. Presenting symptoms — nasal obstruction, crusting, epistaxis — are non-specific and mimic CRS. Tissue biopsy showing non-caseating granulomas is required for diagnosis; serum ACE was elevated in only 65% of cases. Systemic corticosteroids are the mainstay; steroid-sparing agents (methotrexate, hydroxychloroquine) used for refractory disease. Sinonasal sarcoidosis should be considered in any patient with atypical or treatment-refractory CRS.

NUT Midline Carcinoma of the Head and Neck: A Systematic Review and Pooled Survival Analysis

A systematic review and pooled survival analysis (Head & Neck; n=138, 85 studies) compiled the world literature on NUT midline carcinoma (NMC) in the head and neck.
