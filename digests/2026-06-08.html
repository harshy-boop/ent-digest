Lateral Wall Versus Perimodiolar Electrodes for Hearing Preservation in Cochlear Implantation: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis.

Eshraghi A et al. … Eshraghi AA (senior, Dept. of Otolaryngology & Cochlear Implant Lab, University of Miami)

Background

Hearing preservation (HP) during cochlear implantation enables acoustic + electric stimulation, improving quality of life. Whether electrode design—lateral wall (LW) vs perimodiolar (PM)—affects HP has been debated due to conflicting individual studies.

Methods

PRISMA-adherent systematic review + random-effects meta-analysis across 5 databases (Jan 2018–Apr 2025). 76 studies screened; 72 (n=3,783 ears) in meta-analysis. JBI bias assessment; subgroup analysis by follow-up duration; heterogeneity by I².

Results

Pooled HP: LW 45.9% vs PM 34.2% (P<0.000005). In 18 head-to-head studies (n=1,710): RR 1.24 [0.99–1.56], P=0.057. Sensitivity analysis excluding ≤1 month follow-up: RR 1.37 [1.06–1.77], P=0.016 — significant LW superiority.

Limitations

HP definitions vary across studies; mostly retrospective. Overall head-to-head RR achieves significance only after sensitivity analysis. Cochlear anatomy and surgical technique are uncontrolled confounders.

Clinical Takeaway

When hearing preservation is the surgical priority, lateral wall arrays should be the default choice. This meta-analysis is robust enough to influence institutional protocols and informed-consent discussions at cochlear implant programs.

Why score 5: 72-study PRISMA meta-analysis of 3,783 ears in the specialty flagship journal. Sensitivity analysis confirms LW superiority (RR 1.37; P=0.016). Directly practice-changing for CI surgical planning.

Antiviral Prophylaxis for Delayed Facial Nerve Palsy Following Vestibular Schwannoma Resection: Single Institutional Series With Systematic Literature Review and Meta-Analyses of 7136 Patients.

Youssef AS et al. … Youssef AS (senior, Dept. of Neurosurgery, University of Colorado School of Medicine, Anschutz Medical Campus)

Background

Delayed facial nerve palsy (DFNP)—worsening ≥2 HB grades on PODs 5–30—affects ~12% of VS resections. Viral reactivation (HSV/VZV) is implicated; antiviral prophylaxis is inconsistently used due to lack of pooled evidence.

Methods

Two-part study: (1) retrospective institutional cohort of 301 VS resections with pre/post implementation of valacyclovir 1000 mg TID × 7 days; (2) PRISMA meta-analysis of 28 studies (n=6,835) combined with institutional series = 7,136 patients.

Results

Institutional DFNP: 5%; no significant difference valacyclovir vs no-valacyclovir (6% vs 3%; P=0.42). Pooled background DFNP incidence: 12.8%. Meta-analysis of 5 comparative studies: antiviral prophylaxis reduced DFNP risk (RR 0.73 [0.60–0.88]; NNT=16). >80% recovered to HB I–II regardless of treatment.

Limitations

No RCT exists. Only 5 of 28 studies are comparative. Institutional cohort underpowered. High natural recovery rate (>80%) complicates benefit attribution. Publication bias unquantified.

Clinical Takeaway

Pooled data across 7,136 patients support perioperative antiviral prophylaxis (NNT=16, minimal cost/risk). Skull base programs not currently using valacyclovir should consider adopting it pending a definitive RCT.

Why score 5: Largest pooled analysis on VS-related delayed facial palsy (7,136 patients). NNT=16 with a low-risk, inexpensive drug. Practice-adoptable finding for skull base programs while awaiting RCT evidence.

American Thyroid Association 2026 Guidelines for Thyroid Disease in Preconception, Pregnancy, and Postpartum.

ATA Task Force, Shan Z et al.

Background

Thyroid disease is the second most common endocrine disorder in pregnancy. The prior ATA guideline dates from 2017; substantial new evidence has emerged on TSH targets, biologic therapies for Graves' disease, and cancer management in pregnancy.

Methods

Multinational 10-society task force + patient advocates. GRADE-based systematic literature review. Good Practice Statements where RCT data is absent. Clinical questions informed by stakeholder feedback and global needs assessment.

Results

Eight major domains updated: thyroid function testing and screening; iodine supplementation; autoimmunity; hypothyroidism (revised TSH targets); isolated hypothyroxinemia; hyperthyroidism/Graves' disease (new antithyroid drug guidance); thyroid nodules and cancer during pregnancy; postpartum thyroid dysfunction.

Limitations

Much underlying evidence is low-to-moderate quality. TSH reference ranges may vary by assay and trimester. Universal screening remains controversial. Recommendations reflect international consensus requiring local context interpretation.

Clinical Takeaway

All clinicians managing thyroid disease in women of reproductive age must review this 2026 guideline. It supersedes the 2017 ATA document, updates TSH management thresholds, Graves' disease protocols, and nodule surveillance during pregnancy. This is the new standard of care.

Why score 5: Major new ATA CPG replacing the 2017 edition. GRADE methodology, 10-society multinational authorship. Mandatory reading for thyroid surgeons, endocrinologists, and obstetricians managing reproductive-age women.

Recurrence Risk of Differentiated Thyroid Cancer Treated with Total or Hemithyroidectomy. Systematic Review and Meta-analysis.

Trimboli P et al. … Trimboli P (senior, Università della Svizzera Italiana, Lugano)

Background

Hemithyroidectomy (HT) is increasingly used for low-risk differentiated thyroid cancer (DTC). Direct comparisons with total thyroidectomy (TT) recurrence outcomes have been inconsistent.

Methods

PRISMA systematic review of PubMed/MEDLINE and Embase through May 2025. 46 retrospective studies, 98,604 patients (TT n=75,531; HT n=23,073). Primary endpoint: risk difference (RD) in recurrence. Random-effects model; two independent reviewers.

Results

Pooled recurrence: TT 5.8% vs HT 8.1%. HT carried significantly higher risk (OR 1.56 [1.47–1.66]; I²=85%). Absolute RD: +3% for HT (95% CI 3–4%). Consistent in papillary thyroid carcinoma subgroup (OR 1.87; n=14,701).

Limitations

All 46 studies retrospective; likely selection bias (lower-risk tumors tend to receive HT). High heterogeneity (I²=85–87%). Long-term disease-specific survival not captured. RAI eligibility differences not consistently reported.

Clinical Takeaway

When counseling DTC patients on extent of surgery, incorporate a 3% absolute excess recurrence risk with HT. Consistent across 98,604 patients. Patients choosing HT for quality-of-life reasons should be explicitly counseled in the shared decision-making conversation.

Why score 4: Largest meta-analysis on surgical extent in DTC (98,604 patients). Clear signal: OR 1.56 for recurrence with HT vs TT. High heterogeneity limits certainty, but magnitude and consistency are relevant for surgical counseling.

Efficacy of cetuximab-based regimens after immune checkpoint inhibitor failure in recurrent or metastatic head and neck squamous cell carcinoma: a systematic review and meta-analysis.

Yosefof E et al. … Yosefof E (senior, Dept. of Otolaryngology H&N Surgery, Rabin Medical Center / Tel Aviv University)

Background

After ICI failure in R/M HNSCC (pembrolizumab-based first-line), there is no established standard of care. Cetuximab (anti-EGFR) is commonly used but its post-ICI efficacy was unknown.

Methods

PRISMA random-effects meta-analysis; 4 databases (Jan 2014–Jan 2026). Outcomes: ORR, DCR, median PFS, and OS. 14 studies, 411 patients.

Results

Pooled ORR: 47% (95% CI 37–56%). DCR: 70% (58–80%). Median PFS: 4.69 months. Median OS: 10.71 months. Numerically higher ORR with cetuximab+chemo vs monotherapy (not significant). DCR comparable.

Limitations

All studies retrospective; no prospective RCT. Selection bias (fitter patients get second-line). Non-uniform response definitions. Cannot guide biomarker-based patient selection.

Clinical Takeaway

Cetuximab shows meaningful activity post-ICI in R/M HNSCC (ORR ~47%)—higher than assumed. Cetuximab+chemotherapy appears reasonable for platinum-naïve patients. Best available evidence for tumor board discussions while prospective trials are awaited.

Why score 4: First meta-analysis addressing second-line therapy after ICI failure in R/M HNSCC—a common clinical scenario with no established standard. ORR 47% and OS 10.71 months directly inform tumor board decision-making.
